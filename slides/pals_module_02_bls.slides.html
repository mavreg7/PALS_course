Arrest Recognition

The 10-Second Window

⚠️ Action Sequence
1
Verify scene safety before approaching
2
Tap and shout to assess responsiveness
3
Shout for help · activate ERS · get AED
4
Simultaneously check breathing AND pulse for ≥10 s
5
No pulse or HR <60 with poor perfusion → start CPR
💉 Pulse Check Site
  • Infant (<1 year): brachial artery (inner upper arm)
  • Child / Adolescent: carotid or femoral artery
  • Cannot confidently confirm pulse within 10 s? Assume arrest → begin CPR
🟡 HR <60 + poor perfusion

A detectable but bradycardic heart rate (<60/min) with signs of poor perfusion is treated as cardiac arrest — start compressions and ventilations.

🔴 2025 Change: Simultaneous assessment

Previous guidelines asked rescuers to check breathing first, then pulse, sequentially. The 2025 update mandates both simultaneously in ≥10 seconds to reduce time to first compression. Train this as a single action, not two.

🔴 Avoid these common errors

Excessive ventilation (raises intrathoracic pressure, reduces coronary perfusion) · incomplete recoil (leaning on chest) · too-rapid rate >120/min (reduces filling time) · long pauses · inadequate depth. Feedback devices significantly improve performance.

Compression Technique

Method & Ratio by Age

Age1-Rescuer technique2-Rescuer techniqueRatioHand position
Neonate 2-thumb encircling hands preferred 2-thumb encircling hands 3:1 Lower ⅓ sternum
Infant (<1 yr) 2 fingers, just below nipple line 2-thumb encircling hands (preferred) 30:2 / 15:2 Lower ⅓ sternum
Child (1 yr – puberty) 1 or 2 hands, heel of hand 2 hands, heel of hand 30:2 / 15:2 Lower half sternum · avoid xiphoid
Adolescent 2 hands, adult technique 2 hands 30:2 Centre of chest, lower sternum

🟢 2-Rescuer infant advantage

The 2-thumb encircling hand technique generates higher peak systolic and coronary perfusion pressures than the 2-finger method. When 2 rescuers are present for an infant, always use it. The encircling hands also provide tactile feedback on recoil.

🟡 Switch compressors every 2 min

Compression quality degrades significantly within 2 minutes due to fatigue — even when the rescuer feels capable of continuing. Plan role switches at every rhythm check. Announce “switch” with 5 compressions remaining to allow a seamless transition.

Defibrillation

AED Use — Defibrillate Early When Shockable

⚡ Pad Selection
  • <8 years or <25 kg: paediatric pads or attenuator cable
  • ≥8 years or ≥25 kg: adult pads
  • If only adult pads available for infant/child: use them — do not delay shock
  • Anterior-anterior or anterior-posterior placement both acceptable
  • Ensure pads do not touch each other
📲 AED Workflow
1
Power on AED and follow voice prompts
2
Place pads (anterior-anterior or A-P)
3
Clear patient · allow analysis
4
Deliver shock if advised — ensure all clear
5
Resume CPR immediately — no pulse check after shock

🔴 No pulse check after shock

The 2025 guidelines are explicit: resume CPR immediately after shock delivery without pausing for a pulse check. Even a successful defibrillation takes 1–2 minutes of CPR to restore adequate perfusion. The rhythm check happens after the next 2-minute CPR cycle.

Knowledge Check

Tap each card to reveal the answer

Question 1

Two rescuers are present for a 7-month-old in cardiac arrest. What is the preferred compression technique and ratio?

Tap to reveal →

2-thumb encircling hands technique at the lower ⅓ sternum. With 2 healthcare provider rescuers, the ratio is 15:2. The 2-thumb technique generates superior coronary perfusion pressure compared with 2-finger compression and is the preferred 2-rescuer method for infants.

Question 2

A 5-year-old has a pulse of 52/min with mottled skin, weak central pulses, and altered consciousness. What do you do?

Tap to reveal →

Start CPR. A heart rate <60/min with signs of poor perfusion is treated as cardiac arrest in paediatric BLS. Do not wait for pulselessness. Give compressions at a 30:2 ratio (1 rescuer) or 15:2 (2 rescuers) and provide ventilations.

Question 3

What is the maximum acceptable pause in chest compressions, and when does this most commonly occur?

Tap to reveal →

Maximum pause is <10 seconds. It most commonly occurs during rhythm checks, pulse checks, and intubation attempts. Compressions should restart immediately after a shock is delivered, without pausing for a pulse check, and should continue during preparation for intubation.

Question 4

You arrive to a 3-year-old in arrest and the only AED available has adult pads. What do you do?

Tap to reveal →

Use the adult pads. Do not delay defibrillation because paediatric pads are unavailable. Place them anterior-posterior if possible to avoid pad-to-pad contact. Early defibrillation of a shockable rhythm is the single intervention most likely to restore perfusion in VF/pVT.

Clinical Pearl
“Push hard, push fast, don’t lean, count it out loud.”

Compression fraction is the single strongest predictor of survival in paediatric cardiac arrest. Coach your team aloud — rate, depth, recoil, switch. Every two minutes, every time, without exception.
