Post-Resuscitation
Care

ROSC is not the finish line — it's the starting point. Optimizing post-cardiac arrest care determines neurologic outcome and long-term survival.

The Problem
Post-Cardiac Arrest Syndrome
🧠
Brain dysfunction
25% of survivors with "favorable" outcome have global cognitive impairment. 85% of older children have selective neuropsychological deficits at 1-year follow-up.
❤️
Myocardial dysfunction
Transient post-arrest cardiomyopathy is common. Low cardiac output syndrome occurs in 25–50% of survivors. Blood pressure is often labile post-ROSC.
🔥
Ischemia-reperfusion injury
Reperfusion after ischemia generates reactive oxygen species and activates inflammatory cascades — compounding primary brain injury.
⚠️
Secondary injury is preventable
Key secondary injury contributors: hypotension, hypoxia, hyperoxia, hypoglycemia, hyperglycemia, hyperthermia, seizures. ALL are modifiable.
Immediate Priorities
First 30 Minutes After ROSC
1
Airway + ventilation — Assess and secure airway. Target SpO₂ 94–99% (avoid hyperoxia). PaCO₂ 35–45 mmHg (avoid hyper/hypocarbia). Confirm advanced airway with ETCO₂.
2
Hemodynamic stabilization — Maintain systolic BP >10th percentile for age, MAP target per age. Vasopressors ± fluid as needed. Avoid hypotension — directly associated with decreased survival.
3
12-lead ECG — Identify ischemia, ST changes, QTc, arrhythmias. Informs coronary intervention need.
4
Glucose — Check immediately. Treat hypoglycemia (<70 mg/dL). Avoid hyperglycemia (>180 mg/dL). Both extremes worsen neurologic outcome.
5
Temperature — Initiate deliberate temperature control if patient is not following commands. Target 32–37.5°C as soon as possible.
Hemodynamics
Blood Pressure Targets Post-ROSC
🩺
Hypotension is common post-arrest (25–50% of patients) and directly associated with death. Duration of hypotension correlates with decreased survival and worsened neurologic outcome.
📊
Minimum target (2025 guideline)
Systolic BP >10th percentile for age. (COR 1, LOE C-LD). Applies throughout the post-arrest period.
🎯
Optimal target (observational)
Higher survival when systolic BP >10th percentile AND diastolic BP >50th percentile during first 6 hours post-arrest.
💉
Vasopressors
No specific vasopressor is recommended over another. Use epinephrine, norepinephrine, or dopamine based on clinical context and available resources.
💧
Fluid strategy
Cautious fluid resuscitation for true hypovolemia. Avoid fluid overload — post-arrest myocardial dysfunction makes the heart vulnerable to excess preload.
Oxygenation & Ventilation
Respiratory Targets After ROSC
🫁
Oxygenation targets
SpO₂: 94–99%
PaO₂: 60–105 mmHg
Start with 100% FiO₂, then wean once stable monitoring achieved. Avoid hyperoxia — generates reactive oxygen species.
💨
Ventilation targets
PaCO₂: 35–45 mmHg
Avoid hypocapnia (cerebral vasoconstriction) and hypercapnia (cerebral vasodilation, ICP rise).
Use ETCO₂ for continuous trending.
⚠️
Hyperventilation post-ROSC is dangerous. Lowers PaCO₂ → cerebral vasoconstriction → worsens ischemic brain injury. Ventilator rate should maintain normocapnia.
💡
Intra-arrest ETCO₂ monitoring: Values ≥20 mmHg during CPR associated with ROSC and survival. A single low value should NOT be used alone to stop resuscitation.
Temperature Management
Targeted Temperature Management (TTM)
🌡️
TTM = deliberate control of body temperature to reduce secondary neurologic injury. Applies to patients not following commands after ROSC.
🌡️
Temperature target
32–37.5°C. Both hypothermic TTM (32–34°C) and fever prevention (36–37.5°C) are acceptable. Start as soon as possible after ROSC.
⏱️
Duration
Adults: at least 36 hours of temperature control. Pediatric optimal duration is a known knowledge gap — ongoing research needed.
🚫
Avoid fever
Hyperthermia post-ROSC independently worsens neurologic outcome. Active fever prevention is critical even if cooling below 36°C is not pursued.
⚠️
Who qualifies?
Comatose after ROSC (not following verbal commands). If patient is alert and following commands — TTM is not indicated.
Seizure Management
Post-Arrest Seizures
🧠
Post-cardiac arrest seizures increase cerebral metabolic demand, worsen secondary brain injury, and may be non-convulsive (clinically silent). Active monitoring is required.
1
EEG monitoring — Obtain EEG in all comatose post-arrest patients. (COR 2a, LOE C-LD). Non-convulsive status epilepticus can only be detected by EEG.
2
Treat clinical seizures — Benzodiazepines first line, escalate to levetiracetam/phenytoin/fosphenytoin/phenobarbital per status epilepticus protocol.
3
Seizure prophylaxis? — Known knowledge gap. No clear evidence that prophylactic antiepileptics improve outcomes post-arrest. Currently not routinely recommended.
4
Myoclonus without EEG correlate — Do NOT treat automatically. Treatment may cause harm without benefit. Requires EEG characterization first.
Glucose & Metabolic
Glucose, Lactate & Metabolic Targets
⬇️
Avoid hypoglycemia
Target glucose >70 mg/dL. Hypoglycemia worsens neuronal ischemia. Check frequently, especially in infants and young children.
⬆️
Avoid hyperglycemia
Target glucose <180 mg/dL. Hyperglycemia promotes osmotic injury and oxidative stress post-arrest. Tight control not recommended — risk of hypoglycemia.
🧪
Lactate clearance
Serial lactate measurements reflect tissue perfusion. Declining lactate = improving perfusion and cardiac output post-ROSC.
💡
Post-arrest care involves multiple simultaneous physiologic targets. A standardized post-cardiac arrest care bundle improves adherence and outcomes.
→
Consider antibiotics if infection was contributing cause of arrest
→
Identify and treat underlying etiology — arrests don't occur in isolation
Neuroprognostication
Predicting Neurologic Outcome
🧠
2025 First time: PALS guidelines include neuroprognostication recommendations. Multimodal approach required — no single test is sufficient. (COR 1, LOE C-LD)
⏱️
Timing
Delayed impression: ≥72 hours from ROSC (or from achieving normothermia). Earlier assessments are unreliable due to sedation, cooling, and ongoing injury.
📊
EEG
Reasonable to use EEG up to 72h post-arrest to support favorable or unfavorable neurological prognosis. Must be combined with other modalities. (COR 2a)
🔍
Neurologic examination
Cough/gag reflex and response to pain — usefulness to support prognosis is NOT well established in isolation. Cannot be used alone. (2025 New)
🧬
Biomarkers
Neurofilament light chain (NfL) added in 2025 as serum biomarker. Neuro-specific enolase and other biomarkers may supplement multimodal assessment.
Recovery
Survivorship & Long-Term Outcomes
🌱
Recovery from cardiac arrest extends far beyond hospital discharge. Survivors require integrated medical, rehabilitative, caregiver, and community support.
📋
Pre-discharge assessment
Structured assessment of physical, cognitive, and emotional needs before discharge. Identify rehabilitation requirements early. (COR 2a, LOE C-LD)
🔄
1-year follow-up
Evaluate physical, cognitive, and emotional needs throughout the first year. Neuropsychological assessment especially important in school-age children.
👨‍👩‍👦
Family support
Caregivers experience emotional distress. Structured assessment and treatment/referral for emotional distress before hospital discharge benefits both family and patient.
📊
The numbers
25% of "favorable outcome" survivors have global cognitive impairment. 85% have neuropsychological deficits at 1 year. Rehabilitation matters.
Special Situations
Post-ROSC After Specific Etiologies
💔
Myocarditis / Cardiomyopathy
Transfer early to ECMO/MCS center. High probability of myocardial recovery. Rhythm monitoring critical — ventricular arrhythmias persist post-ROSC.
🫁
Asthma / Respiratory arrest
Reduce ventilator rates to prevent air trapping. Target PaCO₂ 40–45 or higher (permissive hypercapnia). Bronchodilators continued post-ROSC.
💊
Toxin-induced arrest
Continue specific antidote infusions (e.g., high-dose insulin for CCB, lipid emulsion for LA toxicity, naloxone for opioid). Recurrence risk persists until drug cleared.
🔧
Single ventricle CHD
Post-ROSC hemodynamic management differs significantly. Early transfer to cardiac center. Standard vasopressor targets may not apply. Cardiology input essential.
Monitoring Bundle
Post-Cardiac Arrest Monitoring Checklist
Continuous monitoring
Pulse oximetry (SpO₂ 94–99%)
ETCO₂ / PaCO₂ (35–45 mmHg)
Cardiac monitor (arrhythmia surveillance)
Invasive arterial BP if available
Temperature monitoring (core)
EEG (in comatose patients)
Intermittent / diagnostic
Serial glucose checks
Lactate (serial clearance)
Electrolytes (K⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺)
12-lead ECG
Echocardiography (myocardial function)
Head CT / MRI (at 72h+ for prognosis)
Family Care
Family Presence During Resuscitation & After
💚
Family presence during CPR reduces complicated grief and improves psychological outcomes for family members. Institutions should have policies supporting this practice.
👨‍👩‍👧
During resuscitation
Dedicated support person for family. Institutions should develop policies defining circumstances where presence may be limited and ensure adequate support resources.
💬
Communication post-ROSC
Honest, timely, and compassionate briefings. Discuss prognosis uncertainty early. Shared decision-making for ongoing care using patient goals and values.
🧩
Caregiver distress
Structured assessment and referral for emotional distress before hospital discharge is recommended. Studies show benefit of psychosocial intervention for survivor-caregiver pairs.
🔄
Team debriefing
Both immediate (hot) and delayed (cold) debriefing are recommended. 2025 New: Different debriefing types identify different improvement opportunities.
Clinical Scenario
Managing Post-Arrest Care
🏥 Scenario
Following 18 minutes of CPR for asystole, a 6-year-old (22 kg) achieves ROSC. She is now intubated. HR 110, BP 55/30 (for age: 10th percentile systolic is ~85), SpO₂ 100% on FiO₂ 1.0, ETCO₂ 48. Temperature 38.6°C. Blood glucose 42 mg/dL. She does not respond to voice.
🚨 Critical findings
Hypotension (BP below 10th percentile), hypoglycemia (42 mg/dL), hyperthermia (38.6°C), possible hyperoxia (SpO₂ 100% on FiO₂ 1.0)
Immediate actions
1. Dextrose 0.5 g/kg IV (10% dextrose 5 mL/kg). 2. Start vasopressor (epinephrine or norepinephrine). 3. Wean FiO₂ to SpO₂ target 94–99%.
Temperature management
Start TTM now — target 36–37.5°C. Patient is comatose post-ROSC. Active cooling to prevent fever. Antipyretics and cooling blanket.
Next steps
12-lead ECG, EEG monitoring (comatose), serial glucose checks, lactate, echocardiography, family communication, PICU transfer.
