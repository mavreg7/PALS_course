🟣 Cuffed vs uncuffed

2025 PALS recommends cuffed ETTs for all age groups including infants. Maintain cuff pressure 20–25 cmH₂O. Cuffed tubes reduce the need for repeated exchanges and provide better seal for positive pressure ventilation.

Intubation

ETT Sizing & Insertion Depth

📝 Internal Diameter (mm)
  • Cuffed ETT: (age ÷ 4) + 3.5 mm
  • Uncuffed ETT: (age ÷ 4) + 4.0 mm
  • Neonate (term): 3.0–3.5 mm
  • Preterm <34 wk: 2.5 mm
  • Verify with Broselow tape when available
  • Have one size larger and one smaller ready at all times
📍 Insertion Depth at Lip (cm)
  • Age-based: (age ÷ 2) + 12 cm
  • Tube-based: ETT size × 3
  • Neonate: 6 + weight (kg) cm
  • Confirm with equal bilateral breath sounds
  • CXR after stabilisation: tip 2 cm above carina

🟣 Right mainstem trap

The right mainstem bronchus angles more acutely — an ETT advanced too far will almost always go right. If left side is quiet post-intubation, withdraw tube 1 cm at a time until bilateral sounds are equal.

🔵 Cuff pressure

Check cuff pressure with a manometer: target 20–25 cmH₂O. Over-inflation compresses the tracheal mucosa and causes ischaemic injury within hours.

Placement Confirmation

5-Point Check & DOPES Mnemonic

1
Visualise the cords during laryngoscopy — confirm tube passing between the vocal cords
2
Continuous waveform ETCO₂ — gold standard; 3–5 consistent waveforms confirm tracheal placement
3
Equal bilateral breath sounds on auscultation; no epigastric gurgling with ventilation
4
Symmetric chest rise with each breath; condensation visible in the tube
5
Chest X-ray after stabilisation — tip should be 2 cm above the carina

🔴 DOPES — sudden post-intubation deterioration

Displacement (tube out or in mainstem)  ·  Obstruction (secretions, kink)  ·  Pneumothorax  ·  Equipment failure (vent disconnect, O₂ supply)  ·  Stacked breaths / auto-PEEP. Always disconnect from vent and bag manually to differentiate patient from machine problem.

Rapid Sequence Intubation

The 7 Ps — Never Skip a Step

P
Preparation
SOAP-ME checklist · team briefing · Plan B and C defined before first attempt
P
Preoxygenation
100% NRB mask ≥ 3 min · NC at 4–6 L/min during apnoeic phase extends safe apnoea time
P
Pre-treatment
Treat hypotension first · atropine 0.02 mg/kg for children <1 yr if using succinylcholine
P
Paralysis + Induction
Induction: ketamine 1–2 mg/kg (haemodynamically unstable) or etomidate 0.3 mg/kg  ·  Paralytic: rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg or succinylcholine 1–2 mg/kg
P
Positioning
Sniffing position (head slightly extended) · shoulder roll for infants · ramp obese patients
P
Placement + Proof
Visualise cords · tube passes between them · waveform ETCO₂ confirms · 5-point check
P
Post-intubation Management
Sedation + analgesia infusion · ventilator settings (TV 6–8 mL/kg IBW) · confirm depth on CXR · NG/OG tube · document tube size and depth in notes

Knowledge Check

Tap each card to reveal the answer

Question 1

A 4-year-old needs intubation. What size cuffed ETT should you use, and at what lip depth?

Tap to reveal →

ETT size: (4÷4) + 3.5 = 4.5 mm cuffed.  ·  Insertion depth: (4÷2) + 12 = 14 cm at lip, or 4.5 × 3 = 13.5 cm. Have 4.0 and 5.0 sizes ready. Confirm with waveform ETCO₂ and bilateral breath sounds.

Question 2

Post-intubation, SpO₂ drops from 99% to 76%. ETCO₂ waveform disappears. First step?

Tap to reveal →

Think DOPES. First: disconnect from ventilator and bag manually (rules out equipment failure). Then: check for Displacement (listen for sounds), Obstruction (suction), Pneumothorax (unequal sounds, hypotension). ETCO₂ loss = tube may be displaced or in oesophagus.

Question 3

You're about to give succinylcholine to a 9-month-old for RSI. What pre-treatment should you consider?

Tap to reveal →

Atropine 0.02 mg/kg IV (minimum 0.1 mg) before succinylcholine in children <1 year. Succinylcholine can trigger profound bradycardia via muscarinic stimulation in infants. Atropine prevents this vagal response.

Question 4

For a first-dose adenosine in SVT, what is the dose and how must it be given?

Tap to reveal →

0.1 mg/kg (max 6 mg) — given as a rapid IV push into the most proximal available access, immediately followed by a 10 mL NS flush. Adenosine has a half-life of <10 seconds — slow administration renders it ineffective before it reaches the AV node.

Clinical Pearl
“Plan the airway, then plan the rescue.”

Always define your Plan B and Plan C before you push the paralytic. A working LMA at arm's reach, a surgeon aware of the situation, and a clear surgical airway exit plan are what separate a difficult intubation from a cannot-intubate-cannot-oxygenate crisis.
