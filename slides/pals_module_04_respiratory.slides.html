Module 04
Respiratory Emergencies

Recognizing & Managing Respiratory Failure in Children.

Respiratory failure is the single most common cause of pediatric cardiac arrest. Early recognition of the four categories of respiratory dysfunction — and targeted intervention before decompensation — is the core skill of this module.

14 slides ~25 min AHA 2025
Why It Matters

Most pediatric arrests are respiratory in origin

Unlike adults — where cardiac arrhythmia is the primary arrest mechanism — the majority of pediatric cardiac arrests are preceded by progressive respiratory failure or shock. By the time a child becomes pulseless, severe hypoxia and acidosis have already been present for minutes.

The cascade

Respiratory distress → Respiratory failure → Hypoxia + Hypercarbia → Acidosis → Cardiac arrest

The opportunity

Intervening at respiratory distress or failure — before arrest — dramatically improves survival. This is PALS's core prevention paradigm.

SpO₂ target during resuscitation

Target SpO₂ 94–99%. Avoid hyperoxia after ROSC — titrate oxygen down once circulation is restored.

CO₂ matters too

Hypercarbia causes pulmonary vasoconstriction, acidosis, and worsening respiratory mechanics. Hypocarbia causes cerebral vasoconstriction. Target normocapnia during stabilization.

Classification

Four categories of respiratory dysfunction

The AHA PALS framework organizes all pediatric respiratory problems into four mechanistic categories. Identifying the category guides treatment — the wrong intervention (e.g., bronchodilator for a child with disordered control of breathing) will not help.

1. Upper Airway Obstruction

Examples: croup, epiglottitis, foreign body aspiration, peritonsillar abscess, anaphylaxis, subglottic stenosis.

Sound clue: Inspiratory stridor — turbulent flow through a narrowed upper airway.

2. Lower Airway Obstruction

Examples: asthma, bronchiolitis, foreign body in bronchus.

Sound clue: Expiratory wheeze — air trapping and dynamic collapse during exhalation.

3. Lung Tissue Disease

Examples: pneumonia, pulmonary edema, ARDS, pulmonary contusion.

Sound clue: Crackles (rales) — fluid or exudate in alveolar spaces. Grunting — patient auto-PEEPs to maintain FRC.

4. Disordered Control of Breathing

Examples: seizures, opioid/sedative overdose, raised ICP, neuromuscular disease.

Sound clue: May be silent — irregular rate, apnea, bradypnea with normal-sounding lungs.

Red flag: Bradypnea in a previously tachypneic child

A slowing respiratory rate is a pre-arrest sign — respiratory muscle fatigue. Do NOT interpret as improvement. Bradypnea + bradycardia = impending arrest. Act immediately.

Assessment

Structured respiratory assessment

Combine the Pediatric Assessment Triangle (PAT) with the primary ABCDE survey for a complete picture in under 60 seconds.

PAT — Appearance

  • Tone, interactivity, consolability
  • Gaze — does the child track you?
  • Speech or cry — strong, weak, absent
  • Movement — purposeful vs. limp

PAT — Work of Breathing

  • Retractions: suprasternal, subcostal, intercostal, tracheal tug
  • Nasal flaring (especially in infants)
  • Grunting — expiratory auto-PEEP to maintain FRC
  • Head bobbing in infants
  • Abnormal sounds: stridor, wheeze, gurgling

PAT — Circulation to Skin

  • Pallor, mottling, cyanosis
  • Central cyanosis (lips, tongue) = severe hypoxia

Primary ABCDE — Breathing (B)

  • Rate vs. age-normal
  • Depth — equal air entry?
  • SpO₂ on room air and with O₂
  • ETCO₂ if intubated

Resistance vs. Compliance

Resistance ↑ (obstruction): high peak pressures, noisy breathing, prolonged inspiratory or expiratory phase.

Compliance ↓ (stiff lung — parenchymal disease): needs higher pressure to inflate, reduced tidal volume for same effort, grunting.

Identifying which mechanism predominates directs treatment: bronchodilators for resistance; PEEP/CPAP for compliance.

Category 1

Upper Airway Obstruction — Recognition & Management

Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis)

Cause: Viral (parainfluenza) subglottic edema. Peak age 6 months–3 years.

  • Barky, seal-like cough; inspiratory stridor
  • Nebulized epinephrine 1:1000 — 0.5 mg/kg (max 5 mg) over 5 min
  • Dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg PO/IM/IV — reduces edema, prevents rebound
  • Observe ≥2 h after epinephrine for rebound stridor
  • Heliox may reduce turbulence in severe cases

Anaphylaxis — Airway

Oropharyngeal and laryngeal edema can close the airway within minutes.

  • Epinephrine 1:1000 — 0.01 mg/kg IM (anterolateral thigh; max 0.5 mg) — first and most critical drug
  • EpiPen: 0.15 mg if <30 kg · 0.3 mg if ≥30 kg
  • Repeat every 5–15 min if no response
  • Prepare for early intubation — progressive edema may prevent intubation if delayed

Foreign Body — Partial Obstruction

  • Allow child to maintain preferred position (tripod / sniffing)
  • Minimize agitation — crying worsens obstruction
  • Blow-by oxygen; do NOT perform blind finger sweeps
  • ENT / bronchoscopy for retrieval

Foreign Body — Complete Obstruction

  • Infant (<1 yr): 5 back blows + 5 chest thrusts, alternating
  • Child (≥1 yr): Abdominal thrusts (Heimlich) until expelled
  • If child becomes unconscious → CPR; look for object before each rescue breath

Positioning first

Head-tilt chin-lift or sniffing position. Trauma: jaw thrust without head tilt. These maneuvers alone may relieve partial obstruction.

Category 2

Lower Airway Obstruction — Asthma & Bronchiolitis

Asthma — Mild to Moderate

  • Expiratory wheeze ± prolonged expiratory phase
  • Albuterol (Salbutamol) 2.5 mg nebulized q20 min × 3 doses — first-line bronchodilator
  • Ipratropium bromide 0.25 mg nebulized — add for moderate–severe episodes
  • Methylprednisolone 2 mg/kg IV/PO — reduces airway inflammation
  • SpO₂ target ≥94% on supplemental O₂

Asthma — Severe / Status Asthmaticus

  • Continuous nebulized albuterol
  • Magnesium sulfate 20–50 mg/kg IV over 15–20 min (max 2 g) — smooth muscle relaxation
  • Epinephrine 0.01 mg/kg IM if critical
  • Consider CPAP / BiPAP before intubation
  • Intubation is high risk in status asthmaticus — auto-PEEP can cause hemodynamic collapse. Have needle decompression ready.

Bronchiolitis (RSV)

  • Mostly infants under 2 years; RSV most common pathogen
  • Subcostal retractions, wheeze, crackles, apnea in young infants
  • Supportive only: High-flow nasal cannula O₂ — target SpO₂ ≥94%
  • Bronchodilators NOT routinely recommended (poor response)
  • Suction secretions; CPAP if escalating

Air Trapping — Key Ventilation Risk

Dynamic hyperinflation during bag-mask or mechanical ventilation:

  • Use slow respiratory rate — allow full exhalation (I:E ratio 1:3 or longer)
  • Avoid high tidal volumes — barotrauma risk
  • Sudden hemodynamic collapse in ventilated asthmatic → suspect tension pneumothorax
  • Needle thoracostomy: 2nd ICS midclavicular line
Category 3

Lung Tissue Disease — Pneumonia, Edema, ARDS

Parenchymal disease impairs gas exchange at the alveolar membrane. Compliance is reduced — more pressure is required to inflate the lung. Classic sign: grunting (partial glottic closure on exhalation = patient-generated PEEP to prevent alveolar collapse).

Pneumonia

  • Fever, tachypnea, focal crackles
  • Dullness to percussion over consolidation
  • CXR: infiltrate or consolidation
  • Antibiotics per suspected pathogen
  • Viral (RSV, influenza): supportive O₂ only
  • CPAP if hypoxia persists despite O₂

Pulmonary Edema

  • Bilateral crackles, frothy secretions, tachycardia
  • Cardiogenic: diuretics, CPAP, inotropes (dopamine/dobutamine)
  • Non-cardiogenic (ARDS): lung-protective ventilation
  • Gallop rhythm + hepatomegaly → heart failure origin

ARDS

  • Diffuse bilateral infiltrates, refractory hypoxemia
  • PaO₂/FiO₂ <200 (severe) — requires ABG
  • Lung-protective ventilation: Vt 6 ml/kg IBW, titrate PEEP
  • Prone positioning in severe ARDS
  • Target SpO₂ 94–99%; avoid hyperoxia post-ROSC

ABG quick interpretation

pH <7.35 = acidosis · PCO₂ >45 = respiratory acidosis (hypoventilation) · PCO₂ <35 = respiratory alkalosis (hyperventilation) · PO₂ <60 on room air = significant hypoxia · Combined respiratory + metabolic acidosis = severe decompensation requiring immediate intervention.

Category 4

Disordered Control of Breathing

These patients often have normal-sounding airways and lungs but are not breathing adequately — drive is impaired centrally or peripherally. The danger: low WOB appearance may be falsely reassuring.

Common causes

  • CNS: seizures (post-ictal apnea), raised ICP, meningitis, encephalitis
  • Toxins: opioids, benzodiazepines, anticonvulsants (carbamazepine, diazepam)
  • Metabolic: severe hypoglycemia, electrolyte disturbance
  • Neuromuscular: Guillain-Barré, botulism, spinal cord injury

Clinical pattern

  • Irregular or slow respiratory rate (bradypnea)
  • Low or absent muscle tone
  • Reduced gag reflex — aspiration risk
  • Normal or quiet air entry — NOT wheezy
  • SpO₂ may drop silently without tachypnea
  • Altered consciousness disproportionate to WOB

Management

  • BVM support immediately if effort is inadequate
  • Treat reversible cause first:
  • Hypoglycemia → dextrose (0.5–1 g/kg IV/IO)
  • Opioid overdose → naloxone 0.01 mg/kg IV/IO/IM (max 2 mg)
  • Active seizure → diazepam / lorazepam
  • Early intubation if cause is not rapidly reversible

Key distinguisher

A child with disordered control often does not appear to be working hard to breathe — because they are not. Their drive is absent or severely blunted.

Do not be reassured by a calm, quiet, non-retracting child who is bradypneic and poorly responsive. Monitor SpO₂ and ETCO₂ continuously.

Blow-by oxygen

Blow-by (holding mask near face) delivers unpredictable FiO₂ and is not recommended. A non-rebreather mask held loosely by a parent is more effective and less frightening for a young child.

HFNC in bronchiolitis

High-flow nasal cannula provides modest continuous distending pressure, reduces nasopharyngeal dead space, and improves mucociliary clearance. It is now first-line escalation for bronchiolitis with moderate hypoxia.

BVM Ventilation

Bag-Mask Ventilation — technique and pitfalls

BVM is the most important airway skill in PALS. AHA 2025 guidelines confirm that for out-of-hospital cardiac arrest, BVM achieves similar neurologic outcomes to advanced airway insertion and is preferable for most prehospital providers (COR 2a, LOE C-LD).

Correct technique

  • Position: neutral/sniffing — ear canal level with anterior shoulder
  • E-C clamp: thumb + index form C around mask; remaining 3 fingers lift jaw (E)
  • 2-person technique preferred for difficult airways
  • Rate: 12–20/min with spontaneous rhythm · 20–30/min with advanced airway during CPR
  • Tidal volume: visible chest rise — do not over-inflate
  • Mask size: covers nose and mouth only — not eyes, not under chin

Pitfalls to avoid

  • Overventilation: high rate/volume → air trapping, ↓ venous return, hemodynamic compromise
  • Poor seal: check finger position; facial hair and infant anatomy challenge seal
  • Gastric inflation: vomiting and aspiration risk; use cricoid pressure cautiously in children
  • Head hyperextension in infants: closes the airway — use neutral sniffing position
  • Neck flexion: also closes airway — roll under shoulders in infants to achieve neutral alignment

DOPE — for the intubated patient who suddenly deteriorates

Displacement of tube · Obstruction (secretions, kinking, biting) · Pneumothorax · Equipment failure. Check in this order. Sudden high-pressure alarm + absent breath sounds + hemodynamic collapse = tension pneumothorax until proven otherwise.

Advanced Airway

Endotracheal intubation — indications, sizing, confirmation

Indications

  • Respiratory failure not responding to non-invasive support
  • Inability to protect airway (absent gag, GCS <8)
  • Impending airway loss (progressive angioedema, epiglottitis)
  • Anticipated deterioration during transport
  • Ongoing cardiac arrest requiring CPR

ETT sizing

  • Cuffed (preferred ≥age 2): ID (mm) = (age ÷ 4) + 3.5
  • Uncuffed: ID (mm) = (age ÷ 4) + 4.0
  • Newborn: 3.0–3.5 mm · Infant: 3.5–4.0 mm
  • Insertion depth (cm at lip) ≈ ID × 3
  • Have one size larger and one size smaller available
  • Cuffed tubes preferred — reduce reintubation and improve ventilation control

Confirming placement

  • Primary: ETCO₂ — colorimetric or continuous waveform capnography (mandatory in all settings)
  • Clinical: bilateral equal breath sounds, symmetric chest rise, no epigastric sounds
  • CXR: tip at mid-trachea (T2–T3), above carina
  • Flat ETCO₂ waveform after intubation → esophageal intubation until proven otherwise

Peri-intubation bradycardia

  • Pre-oxygenate aggressively before every attempt
  • Limit laryngoscopy to <30 sec; abort if SpO₂ <90%
  • Atropine 0.02 mg/kg IV/IO (min 0.1 mg) — consider for infants; not routine for all children
  • Vagal stimulation during laryngoscopy is the most common cause
  • LMA (supraglottic airway) as bridge when intubation is difficult or failed
Clinical Scenarios

Apply the framework — rapid scenario review

A

8-month-old · barky cough · inspiratory stridor · RR 48 · SpO₂ 92%

Category: Upper airway obstruction — croup. Action: Blow-by humidified O₂ → Nebulized epinephrine 0.5 mg/kg (max 5 mg) → Dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg PO/IM. Observe ≥2 h for rebound. Minimize agitation. If worsening: prepare for intubation (smaller tube than expected — subglottic edema).

B

5-year-old · expiratory wheeze · RR 38 · SpO₂ 89% · anxious

Category: Lower airway obstruction — acute asthma. Action: High-flow O₂ → Albuterol 2.5 mg nebulized q20 min × 3 → Ipratropium 0.25 mg nebulized → Methylprednisolone 2 mg/kg IV. No improvement: MgSO₄ 40 mg/kg IV over 20 min → Consider CPAP. Intubation last resort — high auto-PEEP risk.

C

3-year-old · grunting · bilateral crackles · RR 58 · SpO₂ 88% · fever 39.7°C

Category: Lung tissue disease — pneumonia. Action: O₂ to target SpO₂ ≥94% → CPAP if severe retractions → Antibiotics → ABG: pH 7.28, PCO₂ 60, PO₂ 60 → Hypercapnic failure → Intubate, lung-protective settings (low Vt, titrated PEEP).

D

12-year-old · post-seizure · RR 6 · shallow · SpO₂ 79% · limp · on carbamazepine

Category: Disordered control of breathing — anticonvulsant toxicity + post-ictal state. Action: BVM immediately (do not wait) → Check glucose → Head-tilt chin-lift → If inadequate despite BVM: intubate → Maintain SpO₂ 94–99%. Treat cause.

Key Takeaways

Module 04 — Core principles

1. Categorize before treating

Upper · Lower · Lung tissue · Disordered control. Each has distinct physiology and distinct first-line treatment. Misidentification wastes critical time.

2. SpO₂ target: 94–99%

Below = hypoxia requiring action. Above = unnecessary risk of oxygen toxicity post-ROSC. Titrate oxygen delivery continuously and deliberately.

3. Grunting and bradypnea are danger signs

Grunting = parenchymal disease + patient fighting alveolar collapse. Bradypnea in a previously tachypneic child = muscle fatigue + impending arrest. Both demand immediate escalation.

4. DOPE when intubated patient deteriorates

Displacement · Obstruction · Pneumothorax · Equipment failure. Rule out tension pneumothorax first in hemodynamic collapse — needle thoracostomy is fast and potentially life-saving.

5. BVM before advanced airway

For most children, skilled bag-mask ventilation achieves equivalent neurologic outcomes to intubation prehospital (AHA 2025, COR 2a). Master the technique. Two-person BVM is better than a poorly performed intubation.

6. Confirm ETT with ETCO₂ — always

Clinical assessment alone misses esophageal intubation. Capnography (colorimetric or waveform) is mandatory for ETT confirmation in every setting, every time.
