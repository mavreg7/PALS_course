Module 04

Respiratory Emergencies

Distress vs failure · BVM · ETCO2 · intubation indications

Objectives

You will be able to

  • Distinguish respiratory distress from respiratory failure.
  • Classify respiratory problems by anatomic site: upper, lower, lung tissue, control of breathing.
  • Choose appropriate oxygen delivery for the level of distress.
  • Perform effective BVM ventilation with proper technique.
  • Identify indications for advanced airway and avoid hyperventilation.

Distress vs Failure

Recognize the Tipping Point

DISTRESS

Increased work, adequate gas exchange

  • Tachypnea, retractions, nasal flaring
  • Mild-moderate hypoxia, normal CO2
  • Alert, responsive
FAILURE

Inadequate gas exchange

  • Bradypnea or agonal breathing, exhaustion
  • Severe hypoxia, rising CO2
  • Decreased LOC, cyanosis, bradycardia

Four Anatomic Categories

Type-Specific Management

Upper airway

Croup, FB, anaphylaxis, retropharyngeal abscess.
Mx: position, racemic epi, dexamethasone, prepare difficult airway team.

Lower airway

Asthma, bronchiolitis.
Mx: albuterol, steroids, mag sulfate, HFNC.

Lung tissue

Pneumonia, ARDS, pulmonary edema.
Mx: O2, antibiotics, CPAP/BiPAP, lung-protective ventilation.

Control of breathing

Sepsis, ICH, intoxication, seizure.
Mx: treat cause, support ventilation, naloxone if opioid.

BVM Technique

The Most Important Pediatric Airway Skill

E-C clamp · 1-rescuer

  • Mask size: bridge of nose to cleft of chin
  • Head-tilt/chin-lift (neutral in infants)
  • Squeeze bag just enough for chest rise
  • 1 breath every 2-3 s (20-30/min) in arrest

2-rescuer · 2-thumb method

  • One operator: mask seal with two hands
  • Second operator: squeezes the bag
  • Preferred when O2 sat is unsatisfactory or seal is difficult
  • Consider OPA (no gag) / NPA

Avoid hyperventilation — raises intrathoracic pressure, drops venous return and coronary perfusion.

ETCO2 & Intubation

Confirm · Monitor · Decide

ETCO2 targets

  • Normal: 35-45 mmHg
  • During CPR: aim ≥15-20 mmHg = adequate cardiac output
  • Sudden drop → loss of circulation or tube dislodged

Intubation indications

  • Cannot oxygenate or ventilate with BVM/NIV
  • Loss of airway reflexes / GCS ≤ 8
  • Anticipated clinical course (e.g., severe burn, sepsis)
  • Anatomic threat (epiglottitis, severe angioedema)

Clinical Pearl

"If the chest rises and the sats climb, you've already won."

Most pediatric arrests start as respiratory. Excellent BVM averts the cardiac arrest. Don't rush to a tube if the mask is working — fix seal, suction, reposition first.

Summary

  • Distress vs failure — recognize the trajectory early.
  • Categorize: upper · lower · lung tissue · control of breathing.
  • BVM technique = the cornerstone skill. Avoid hyperventilation.
  • ETCO2 35-45 normal; ≥15-20 during effective CPR.
  • Intubate for failure of BVM/NIV, lost reflexes, or anticipated course.
