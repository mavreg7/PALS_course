Respiratory Emergencies:
Distress → Failure Happens Fast

Most paediatric arrests start with respiratory compromise. Recognise the pattern early, support oxygenation and ventilation, and intervene before the child tires out.

🫁 Distress vs Failure 🌬 Upper vs Lower Airway 📉 Oxygenation vs Ventilation ⚠️ Red Flags 🃏 Interactive Checks
Core Concept

Children usually deteriorate in a predictable sequence

1

Respiratory distress

Child is compensating — increased work of breathing, tachypnoea, abnormal sounds, but still maintaining oxygenation and mental status.

2

Respiratory failure

Compensation fails — poor air entry, altered mental status, cyanosis, irregular or slowing respiratory rate. This is pre-arrest.

3

Cardiorespiratory arrest

Apnoea or agonal respirations lead to bradycardia and pulseless arrest if untreated. Most paediatric codes start here — not with a primary arrhythmia.

🚨 Clinical Truth

A child who is tiring is more dangerous than a child who is working hard. Decreasing effort can mean worsening failure, not improvement.

Recognition

Respiratory distress vs respiratory failure

Respiratory Distress
  • Tachypnoea
  • Retractions, nasal flaring
  • Stridor or wheeze
  • Anxious but interactive
  • Usually normal or near-normal colour
  • Air entry still present
VS
Respiratory Failure
  • Bradypnoea, irregular breathing, or apnoea
  • Poor or absent air movement
  • Cyanosis or marked pallor
  • Altered mental status, limpness
  • Silent chest or weak cry
  • Need for immediate assisted ventilation
Pattern Recognition

Upper airway, lower airway, lung tissue, or breathing control?

Upper Airway Obstruction

Think: croup, anaphylaxis, foreign body

Stridor, hoarse voice, barky cough, sniffing posture, suprasternal retractions. Problem is usually inspiratory.

Lower Airway Obstruction

Think: asthma, bronchiolitis

Wheeze, prolonged expiratory phase, chest tightness, diffuse air trapping. Problem is usually expiratory.

Lung Tissue Disease

Think: pneumonia, pulmonary oedema

Crackles, tachypnoea, grunting, hypoxia with increased work of breathing. Gas exchange surface is affected.

Disordered Control of Breathing

Think: seizures, CNS injury, intoxication

Irregular breathing, shallow effort, poor respiratory drive. Often minimal work of breathing but inadequate ventilation.

Management Priorities

First actions in paediatric respiratory compromise

1. Position + Oxygen

Let the child stay in the position of comfort when possible. Give high-flow oxygen if hypoxic or severely distressed. Avoid forcing a supine exam on an upper-airway child.

2. Suction + Airway Adjuncts

Clear secretions. Use OPA/NPA only if appropriate. Airway obstruction from secretions is common in infants and tired children.

3. Assist Ventilation Early

If effort is inadequate, start BVM ventilation with 100% O₂. Don't wait for complete apnoea. Use two-person technique if possible.

4. Treat the Cause

Bronchodilator, epinephrine, steroids, foreign-body manoeuvres, antibiotics, glucose, naloxone — management depends on the pattern you identify.

⚠️ Ventilation Matters

Hypoxia is one problem; hypoventilation is another. A child can have a decent SpO₂ briefly and still be retaining CO₂ because they are tiring.

Red Flags

Signs the child is about to crash

Silent Chest

Minimal wheeze in a known asthma patient is not reassuring if air movement is poor.

Decreasing LOC

Agitation → confusion → lethargy is a failure pattern. The brain is no longer being oxygenated adequately.

Bradypnoea

A slowing respiratory rate in a sick child usually means exhaustion, not recovery.

Cyanosis

Late sign of significant hypoxaemia. Do not wait for this before acting.

Poor Tone

Floppy, weak, or weak cry means the child is losing reserve rapidly.

Bradycardia

In paediatrics, bradycardia often means severe hypoxia until proven otherwise.

Cause-Based Pearls

Quick clinical distinctions that change management

Stridor + Drooling

Do not agitate the child

Think critical upper-airway pathology. Keep upright, minimal handling, prepare expert airway help. Forcing an exam may worsen obstruction.

Wheeze + Prolonged Expiration

Think lower airway obstruction

Bronchodilators help, but if the child tires, assisted ventilation becomes more important than repeated nebulisers.

Crackles + Fever

Think lung tissue disease

Pneumonia often causes hypoxia with fast breathing. Oxygenation support and infection treatment matter more than bronchodilators.

Slow, Shallow, Altered

Think central hypoventilation

Prioritise airway protection and ventilation. This child may need BVM or intubation even without dramatic retractions.

Knowledge Check

Tap each card to reveal the answer

Q

A child is working less hard than before, but is now drowsy with poorer air entry. Better or worse?

Tap to reveal →

Worse. Decreasing work of breathing with fatigue, drowsiness, or poor air movement suggests respiratory failure, not recovery.

Q

What sound points most strongly toward upper airway obstruction?

Tap to reveal →

Stridor. Inspiratory noise suggests upper-airway narrowing such as croup, anaphylaxis, or foreign body.

Q

What is the most common cause of paediatric bradycardia in an emergency?

Tap to reveal →

Hypoxia. In children, bradycardia is usually a pre-arrest sign from severe respiratory failure until proven otherwise.

Q

When should you start assisted ventilation?

Tap to reveal →

When effort is inadequate, mental status is worsening, respirations are irregular or slow, or air movement is poor. Do not wait for complete apnoea.

Clinical Pearl
A noisy chest is often safer than a quiet one.

A child with loud wheeze or stridor is still moving air. The child whose sounds are fading, effort is dropping, and mental status is worsening is losing the ability to ventilate. Recognise fatigue early and take over before arrest.

Distress vs Failure

  • Distress = compensating
  • Failure = decompensating / pre-arrest
  • Reduced effort can mean worsening exhaustion
  • Bradycardia in kids often means severe hypoxia

Practical Approach

  • Position, oxygen, suction
  • Recognise upper vs lower vs lung vs central causes
  • Assist ventilation early when effort is inadequate
  • Treat the cause while monitoring for deterioration
