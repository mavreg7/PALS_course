Scenario Setup

9-Month-Old · Septic Shock · 8 kg

Presentation
  • Setting: PICU admit from ED
  • 48 h of fever, decreased PO, diarrhea ×6
  • No allergies · vaccines up to date
  • Blood cultures sent prior to antibiotics
Initial Vitals
  • HR 192 · RR 60
  • BP 68/40 · Temp 39.2°C
  • SpO₂ 92% on room air
  • Cap refill 5 s · cool feet · lethargic
Labs

Lactate: 4.2 mmol/L  |  WBC: 22k with bands  |  Glucose: 58 mg/dL (hypoglycaemia)  |  iCa: 0.9 mmol/L (borderline)

🔴 Trigger Event (at 30 minutes)

After 40 mL/kg fluid (two 20 mL/kg boluses): BP 64/36, patient more mottled, hepatomegaly noted on exam. Cold-shock pattern persists. Escalation to vasoactive therapy required.

Team Structure

Six-Person Resuscitation Team

Team Leader

Drives the sepsis bundle, calls each bolus, orders antibiotics by name and dose, escalates to vasoactive by 60 minutes. Reassesses after every intervention and narrates the mental model aloud.

Airway

Applies supplemental O₂ (NC → HFNC as needed). Monitors closely for impending respiratory failure — septic children deteriorate fast. Ready to escalate to BVM or intubation.

Vascular Access

Inserts two IVs or IO (≤5 min target). Sends blood cultures, CBC, BMP, lactate, blood gas, glucose, iCa. Labels every line. States access site aloud to recorder.

Medications

Draws and labels all fluid boluses, antibiotics, glucose correction, and vasoactive infusion. Double-checks every dose with team leader. Flushes after each drug push.

Recorder

Documents exact times of: bolus #1/#2, antibiotic hung, glucose given, vasoactive started. Calls out elapsed time since sepsis recognition. Alerts team leader at 45 min mark.

Family Liaison

Updates caregivers in real time using plain language. Obtains consent for IO / central line if needed. Keeps family out of the resus bay during critical moments unless requested otherwise.

First-Hour Bundle

Expected Actions — Time-Critical Sequence

0:00
Immediate

Recognize sepsis · O₂ · Monitor · Access ×2

PAT and ABCDE within 60 seconds. Apply O₂ to achieve SpO₂ ≥ 94%. Apply cardiac monitor and pulse oximeter. Two-IV or IO access target within 5 minutes.

0:10
10 minutes

1st Fluid bolus 20 mL/kg (160 mL) over 5–10 min · Glucose correction

Isotonic crystalloid (NS or LR). Glucose: D10W 2–5 mL/kg = 16–40 mL IV. Reassess cap refill, HR, BP, and mental status immediately after each bolus.

0:20
20 minutes

Broad-spectrum antibiotics within 60 min of recognition

Ceftriaxone 50–100 mg/kg IV (400–800 mg). Blood cultures already sent. If immunocompromised: vancomycin + pip-tazo. Confirm allergy history.

0:30
30 minutes

2nd Fluid bolus 20 mL/kg · Reassess for fluid overload

After 40 mL/kg total: check for rales, hepatomegaly, worsening SpO₂. Do not continue boluses if signs of fluid overload appear.

0:40
Critical escalation

Persistent cold shock → Start Epinephrine infusion

Epinephrine 0.05–0.3 mcg/kg/min IV/IO. Peripheral line is acceptable initially. This is NOT a bolus-dose — it's a continuous infusion. Do not confuse with cardiac arrest epinephrine.

0:55
55 minutes

Catecholamine-resistant? → Stress-dose hydrocortisone

Hydrocortisone 2 mg/kg IV (max 100 mg) if no improvement on epinephrine. Obtain central line and arterial line. PICU / intensivist consult if not already placed.

1:00
Goal

Cap refill ≤ 2 s · HR normalising · MAP improving

Lactate trending down. Urine output ≥ 1 mL/kg/h. Continue vasoactive titration. Target normoglycaemia. Reassess q15 min.

Haemodynamic Pattern

Choose the Right Vasoactive — Cold vs Warm Shock

Cold Shock — ↑ SVR
  • Cap refill ≥ 3 s, cool extremities
  • Narrow pulse pressure
  • Mottled, pale skin
  • Most common in infants

First-line: Epinephrine

0.05–0.3 mcg/kg/min. Inotrope + vasoconstrictor. Peripheral IV/IO acceptable until central access obtained.

VS
Warm Shock — ↓ SVR
  • Flash cap refill (< 1 s), warm extremities
  • Wide pulse pressure, bounding pulses
  • Flushed skin
  • More common in older children

First-line: Norepinephrine

0.05–0.3 mcg/kg/min. Predominantly vasopressor. Correct low SVR first; avoid epinephrine (will worsen vasodilation).

⚠️ This patient has COLD shock

Cool feet, prolonged cap refill, narrow pulse pressure, infant age → Epinephrine is the correct first-line vasoactive. Do not choose norepinephrine for this scenario.

Auto-Fail Behaviours

These errors fail the megacode immediately

Antibiotics
  • Delay antibiotics > 60 min from sepsis recognition — each hour of delay increases mortality
  • Not sending blood cultures before antibiotics (cultures invalidated)
  • Wrong antibiotic choice for presumed source
Fluid Management
  • Withholding fluid boluses in the presence of hypoperfusion and no signs of overload
  • Continuing boluses despite rales, hepatomegaly, SpO₂ drop (fluid overload)
  • Not reassessing after each bolus — every bolus must have a defined endpoint
Vasoactives
  • Wrong vasoactive for shock pattern — norepinephrine in cold shock, epinephrine in warm shock
  • Starting vasoactive before correcting hypoglycaemia / hypocalcaemia
  • Giving a cardiac-arrest bolus dose instead of a continuous infusion
Team / Recognition
  • Failing to name "septic shock" within 5 minutes — verbal recognition triggers the bundle
  • No closed-loop on drug orders — silent medication errors go undetected
  • Recorder not tracking elapsed time — team loses situational awareness

Knowledge Check

Tap each card to reveal the answer

Question

The 8 kg child has received 40 mL/kg fluid but BP remains 64/36 and extremities are still cold. What is your next step?

Tap to reveal →

Start epinephrine infusion at 0.05–0.3 mcg/kg/min IV/IO. This patient has fluid-refractory cold shock. Peripheral IV is acceptable initially while central access is being obtained.

Question

What findings on reassessment should make you STOP giving fluid boluses even if BP is still low?

Tap to reveal →

Signs of fluid overload: new rales on auscultation, worsening hepatomegaly, SpO₂ dropping, increasing respiratory distress. At this point, switch to vasoactives rather than continuing fluids.

Question

Why must blood cultures be sent BEFORE starting antibiotics?

Tap to reveal →

Antibiotics sterilize the blood within minutes, making cultures falsely negative. Cultures are essential for identifying the organism and guiding de-escalation. Never delay antibiotics by more than a few minutes to get cultures — but do not skip them entirely.

Question

An older child presents with warm extremities, bounding pulses, and BP 78/30. Which vasoactive do you choose?

Tap to reveal →

Norepinephrine — this is warm shock (distributive, low SVR). Norepinephrine is predominantly a vasopressor that restores vascular tone. Epinephrine would add inotropy but also beta-2 vasodilation, worsening the low-resistance state.

Facilitator Guide

Debrief Prompts — 15 Minutes

Facilitation questions
  • "Walk me through your mental model at the 10-minute mark."
  • "What told you to stop boluses and start a vasoactive?"
  • "When did antibiotics actually get hung — and what slowed you down?"
  • "How did the team reassess after each bolus?"
  • "How did you determine this was cold shock and not warm shock?"
Common themes to surface
  • Antibiotic timing — most teams are late
  • Fluid bolus without reassessment endpoint
  • Confusing infusion epi vs arrest-dose epi
  • Recorder losing time tracking at the vasoactive phase
  • Glucose correction often forgotten under pressure
Clinical Pearl

"Recognize early, treat fast, reassess after every step."

Each paediatric sepsis bundle element saves lives — but only when delivered and reassessed within the first hour. A bolus without a defined reassessment plan is an incomplete intervention. Fix glucose and calcium first — a cold, hypocalcaemic heart will not respond to epinephrine.
