💡 The Core Insight

Unlike adults, most pediatric cardiac arrests are preventable. They begin as respiratory or circulatory failure. Early recognition before arrest is the highest-yield action in pediatric emergency care.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, you will be able to:

🔍

Recognize respiratory distress, failure, shock, and cardiac arrest using a systematic framework (PAT → Primary → Secondary).

⚡

Initiate high-quality CPR, manage airways, and perform defibrillation within 60 seconds of a pediatric cardiac arrest.

💊

Dose and administer resuscitation medications accurately by weight — epinephrine, adenosine, amiodarone, atropine, and more.

📈

Interpret pediatric rhythms and select the correct algorithm: shockable vs non-shockable, tachycardia, bradycardia.

🤝

Lead and participate in resuscitation teams using closed-loop communication, defined roles, and structured debriefs.

🌡

Manage post-ROSC care — targeted temperature management, hemodynamic optimization, and neuroprotection.

2025 AHA Updates

What changed — and why it matters

Updated ✓

IO Access — Now First Line

Intraosseous access is co-equal with IV as first-line vascular access in arrest. Place IO immediately — do not delay drugs seeking an IV.

Updated ✓

CPR Feedback Devices

Real-time CPR feedback devices are strongly recommended for all in-hospital pediatric resuscitations, improving compression quality 15–20%.

Removed ✗

IV Sotalol — Gone

Removed from stable SVT algorithm due to proarrhythmia risk. Amiodarone or procainamide now preferred for refractory SVT.

Strengthened ↑

ECPR Recommendation

Extracorporeal CPR now has a stronger recommendation for in-hospital arrests with a reversible cause when conventional CPR is failing.

Strengthened ↑

Post-ROSC Temperature

Actively prevent fever (>37.5°C) for at least 72 hours post-arrest. TTM at 32–36°C for 48 h in comatose survivors.

Clarified ↔

Epinephrine Timing

Give epi as soon as IO/IV is available in non-shockable arrest. Eliminate delays — every minute without epi worsens outcomes.

Foundation Concept

The Pediatric Chain of Survival

1

Prevention

Most arrests are preventable. Injury prevention, safe sleep, choking education, and early warning systems stop arrest before it starts.

2

Early CPR

Bystander CPR doubles or triples OHCA survival. Every second without CPR causes irreversible neurological injury — start immediately.

3

Rapid Emergency Response

Activate EMS or the code team fast. Assign roles before you start. Time to first shock (if VF) should be under 3 minutes.

4

Advanced Life Support

PALS algorithms, medications, and airway management by a trained team. This is what this course prepares you for.

5

Post-Arrest Care

ROSC is not the finish line. TTM, neuroprotection, hemodynamic support, and early rehabilitation define long-term outcomes.

  • Post-ROSC: prevent fever >37.5°C for ≥72 h
  • CPR feedback devices strongly recommended
