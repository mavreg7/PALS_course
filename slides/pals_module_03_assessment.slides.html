Paediatric Assessment Triangle

Hands-Off, 30-Second First Impression

🔺 Appearance — TICLS

Tone · Interactivity · Consolability · Look/gaze · Speech or cry. Abnormal appearance is the single most sensitive indicator of serious illness in a child.

🌬️ Work of Breathing

Abnormal sounds (stridor, wheeze, grunting) · abnormal positioning (tripod, sniffing) · retractions (subcostal, intercostal, suprasternal) · nasal flaring. Absence of effort in an obtunded child = silent obstruction.

🌡️ Circulation to Skin

Pallor · mottling · cyanosis (central vs peripheral) · capillary refill >2 s. Abnormal skin circulation in the absence of respiratory distress suggests circulatory problem (shock).

🟢 Clinical tip: PAT before you touch

The PAT takes 15–30 seconds and requires no equipment. It immediately classifies the child’s physiological status and tells you whether to intervene now (abnormal appearance + any other abnormal side) or proceed with a structured primary survey.

Primary Assessment

ABCDE — Methodical & Repeatable

A
Airway

Patent / maintainable / not maintainable · reposition, suction, OPA/NPA if needed · consider RSI early if failure

B
Breathing

RR · effort · sounds · SpO₂ (target ≥94%) · chest rise symmetry · ETCO₂ if available

C
Circulation

HR · BP · central + peripheral pulses · cap refill · skin temp/colour · urine output

D
Disability

AVPU or GCS · pupils (size, reactivity, equality) · point-of-care glucose · focal neurological signs

E
Exposure

Core temperature · full skin exam (rash, petechiae, trauma) · expose → assess → cover · prevent hypothermia

💡 A before B before C

Airway problems kill fastest — never proceed to B until A is secured. In paediatrics, airway obstruction from infection (croup, epiglottitis), foreign body, or reduced GCS is common and rapidly fatal. Optimise one step before moving to the next.

The Assessment Cycle

Evaluate → Identify → Intervene → Repeat

🔍 Evaluate

PAT · ABCDE primary survey · vital signs · history (SAMPLE) · focused exam

🎯 Identify

Type of problem: respiratory / circulatory / metabolic / neurological · Severity: stable to arrest

⚡ Intervene

Most time-critical treatment first · airway → O₂ → IV access → fluids → medications · then re-evaluate

↺ Repeat continuously — after every intervention, return to Evaluate

🟢 Continuous reassessment

Children can decompensate rapidly or respond dramatically to treatment. Each reassessment after an intervention confirms whether the problem is resolving or escalating. Never perform an action and walk away — close the loop.

🟠 SAMPLE history

Signs & symptoms · Allergies · Medications · Past history · Last meal · Events leading to presentation. Gather from caregivers simultaneously with the primary survey.

🟡 SBP formula shortcut

For children 1–10 years: minimum acceptable systolic BP = 70 + (2 × age in years). Below this threshold in a child with signs of poor perfusion = hypotensive shock requiring immediate intervention.

🔴 Tachycardia ≠ always pathological

Fever, pain, and anxiety all cause tachycardia. Assess tachycardia in context: if HR is elevated AND perfusion signs are abnormal (cap refill, pulses, skin), treat as circulatory compromise. A falling HR in a shocked child may signal imminent arrest, not improvement.

Clinical State Spectrum

From Stable to Arrest — Intervene Early

►►► Deterioration if untreated ►►►

Stable

Normal PAT · normal vitals · no distress

Respiratory Distress

Increased WOB · normal SpO₂ maintained · alert

Resp Failure / Shock

WOB severe or absent · SpO₂ falling · signs of poor perfusion

Decompensated

Altered consciousness · hypotension · severe hypoxia

Cardiac Arrest

Unresponsive · apnoeic · pulseless · CPR now

🟢 Intervene before decompensation

Children have high physiological reserve and compensate well until they cannot. Respiratory distress and compensated shock are your intervention window. By the time hypotension appears in a child, they are near the edge of cardiac arrest.

🟡 Respiratory vs circulatory primary problem

PAT helps differentiate: abnormal breathing + normal circulation = primary respiratory. Normal breathing + abnormal skin circulation = primary circulatory (shock). Both abnormal = combined or late-stage problem requiring simultaneous intervention on both fronts.

Knowledge Check

Tap each card to reveal the answer

Question 1

A 2-year-old is brought in limp, eyes closed, not crying. Breathing looks laboured with subcostal retractions. Skin is pale. What does the PAT tell you?

Tap to reveal →

All three PAT sides are abnormal: Appearance (limp, unresponsive), Work of Breathing (retractions), Circulation to Skin (pallor). This is a life-threatening emergency — intervene immediately, do not proceed with a leisurely history.

Question 2

In the ABCDE assessment, you note severe stridor and cyanosis. What is your next immediate action?

Tap to reveal →

Address A — Airway first. Reposition the head, suction if secretions present, apply a jaw thrust or chin lift. If not maintainable, insert NPA/OPA. Do not proceed to B until airway is secured. Cyanosis here is a consequence of the obstructed airway, not a primary breathing problem.

Question 3

A 4-year-old weighs 16 kg and has a BP of 76/40. Is this hypotensive?

Tap to reveal →

Minimum SBP for a 4-year-old = 70 + (2 × 4) = 78 mmHg. The measured SBP is 76 — below the lower limit. This is hypotensive shock. Escalate immediately: IV access, fluid bolus (10 mL/kg isotonic), reassess after.

Question 4

You give a fluid bolus to a child in compensated shock. What do you do next?

Tap to reveal →

Return to Evaluate — repeat the PAT, reassess A through E, recheck vital signs. The Evaluate → Identify → Intervene cycle closes only when you confirm the intervention’s effect. Determine if perfusion signs have improved, remained the same, or deteriorated, then identify the next action.

Clinical Pearl
“Treat the trend, not the snapshot.”

A single set of vitals can mislead. The deteriorating child reveals themselves through changing tone, rising work of breathing, falling cap refill, and climbing heart rate — repeated across your assessment cycles. The child who ‘looks better’ after oxygen but has a worsening cap refill is not better.
