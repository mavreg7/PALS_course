Bradycardia Algorithm

Click each step — expand the treatment detail

1

Identify bradycardia with cardiopulmonary compromise

Poor perfusion, hypotension, respiratory distress, or altered consciousness — any of these combined with HR below normal for age.

Clinical markers: pallor, mottling, cap refill >3 sec, irritability or decreased LOC, hypotension for age. If no compromise → observe and treat the cause.
2

Support ABCs — O₂, monitoring, IV/IO access

Open airway, high-flow oxygen, cardiac monitor + pulse oximeter, establish vascular access simultaneously.

Team approach: One person manages airway, one gains IV/IO access, one sets up monitoring — all within 30 seconds. These happen in parallel, not sequence.
3

HR < 60 + poor perfusion despite O₂ & ventilation?

This is the critical branch point. Yes = start CPR immediately. No = treat reversible cause, do not yet perform CPR.

Yes → CPR at 100–120/min, 15:2 ratio with 2-rescuer paediatric CPR. No → continue monitoring, investigate reversible causes, reassess frequently.
4

Epinephrine — first-line drug

Give if bradycardia persists despite adequate oxygenation and ventilation.

0.01 mg/kg IV/IO (0.1 mL/kg of 1:10,000). Repeat every 3–5 min. Max single dose 1 mg.
ET route: 0.1 mg/kg of 1:1,000 (10× higher concentration). Flush with 5 mL NS after.
5

Atropine — vagal or AV block aetiology

Second-line drug. Effective for bradycardia caused by high vagal tone or AV conduction block; not effective for complete heart block.

0.02 mg/kg IV/IO. Minimum dose 0.1 mg (paradoxical bradycardia risk with smaller doses). Maximum single dose 0.5 mg. May repeat once.
Total max dose 1 mg.
6

Treat reversible causes — Hs & Ts

Hypoxia, hypothermia, hypovolaemia, hypo/hyperkalaemia, H⁺ ion — Tension PTX, tamponade, toxins, thromboembolism.

In children, most common are: Hypoxia → fix airway first. Hypovolaemia → 10 mL/kg NS bolus. Hypothermia → active rewarming. Toxins → consider naloxone, atropine, sodium bicarb depending on agent.
7

Consider transcutaneous pacing

For high-degree AV block or sinus node dysfunction not responding to medications.

Start at 100 bpm. Increase milliamps until electrical and mechanical capture. Sedate for pain (midazolam + fentanyl). Arrange transvenous pacing with paediatric cardiology consultation.

⚠️ Amiodarone + Procainamide — NEVER together

Both prolong QT. Combining them dramatically increases risk of torsades de pointes and lethal arrhythmia. Choose one or the other.

Adenosine Technique

Half-life 10 seconds — every second of delivery matters

Setup
  • Use the largest, most proximal IV (antecubital preferred over hand vein)
  • Prepare a 3-way stopcock — adenosine in one port, 10 mL NS flush in the other
  • Both syringes drawn up and labelled before you start
  • Continuous rhythm strip running; team briefed on expected transient asystole
Delivery
  • Push adenosine as fast as physically possible, then immediately flush — close stopcock to patient port and push both syringes simultaneously
  • Elevate the arm to speed delivery to central circulation
  • If no response after 1–2 min → second dose 0.2 mg/kg (max 12 mg)
  • IO route equally effective — same technique applies

⚠️ What to expect on the monitor

Brief sinus pause or asystole lasting seconds. Warn family and team. Have atropine and pacing ready. If pause >10 sec, prepare to pace. The rhythm that follows is diagnostic — sinus tach return confirms the mechanism; a different rhythm reveals the true aetiology.

Energy Selection

Sync or async — choosing the wrong mode can be fatal

⚡ Synchronized Cardioversion

Shock triggered ON the R-wave

  • Use for: Unstable SVT, VT with pulse
  • Starting dose: 0.5–1 J/kg
  • Second shock: 2 J/kg
  • Sedate if time allows (midazolam + fentanyl)
  • Confirm SYNC mode is ON before charging
  • Verify sync markers appear on each QRS peak

🔍 Why sync?

An unsynchronised shock landing on the T-wave can induce VF. Always sync for any organised rhythm with a pulse.

⚡ Defibrillation (Unsynchronised)

Shock delivered immediately

  • Use for: VF, Pulseless VT, Polymorphic VT
  • First dose: 2 J/kg
  • Second dose: 4 J/kg
  • Subsequent: ≥4 J/kg (max 10 J/kg or adult max)
  • Resume CPR immediately after shock — no pause
  • Epinephrine after 2nd shock, then every 3–5 min

⏱ Minimise no-flow time

Continue compressions until defibrillator is charged. Clear patient for ≤1 second. Resume CPR within 5 seconds of delivery.

2025 Guideline Update

IV Sotalol — New Option for Refractory SVT

2025 PALS Guidelines

What changed?

IV sotalol (1 mg/kg over 5 min) is now listed as an acceptable option for stable SVT not responding to adenosine, alongside amiodarone and procainamide — as a class III antiarrhythmic alternative with paediatric evidence.

When to consider

  • SVT refractory to 2 doses of adenosine
  • Atrial flutter/fibrillation in paediatric patients
  • Postoperative junctional ectopic tachycardia (specialist use)
  • Strong evidence from paediatric electrophysiology literature

Monitoring requirements

  • Continuous 12-lead or rhythm monitoring throughout infusion
  • Stop if QTc > 500 ms
  • Blood pressure monitoring — negative inotropic effect
  • Expert consultation strongly recommended before use

🧠 Clinical context

Previously, refractory SVT options were amiodarone (slow infusion, phlebitis risk) or procainamide (hypotension risk). IV sotalol adds a faster-onset class III option with a well-established paediatric safety profile. It does not replace adenosine as first-line — it expands the toolkit for refractory cases.

Knowledge Check

Question

4-year-old, HR 240, no P-waves visible, poor perfusion. Adenosine not immediately available. What energy do you use for cardioversion?

Tap to reveal →

0.5–1 J/kg synchronised. Unstable SVT with compromised perfusion — don't wait for adenosine. Sync cardioversion immediately. Sedate if time permits. Second shock 2 J/kg.

Question

Why is there a minimum dose of 0.1 mg for atropine, regardless of the weight calculation?

Tap to reveal →

Sub-therapeutic doses can cause paradoxical bradycardia by stimulating presynaptic muscarinic receptors. Always give at least 0.1 mg even in very small infants.

Question

You charge the defibrillator and press deliver — nothing happens. The rhythm is VF. What is the most likely explanation?

Tap to reveal →

The defibrillator is in SYNC mode, waiting for an R-wave that doesn't exist in VF. Switch to unsynchronised mode and deliver the shock immediately.

Question

After a 2-second sinus pause post-adenosine, the child converts to sinus tachycardia at HR 160. What does this tell you?

Tap to reveal →

The sinus tach that follows reveals the underlying mechanism was SVT (not sinus tachycardia from the start). Now investigate the cause of the sinus tach — fever, pain, hypovolaemia, anxiety.

Clinical Pearl

"Find the cause, then chase the rhythm."

Sinus tachycardia from fever won't respond to adenosine. Bradycardia from hypoxia won't respond to atropine. Treating only the rhythm without addressing the driver is a recipe for recurrence and failure. Always ask: why is this heart beating like this?
