The First 30 Seconds

The Pediatric Assessment Triangle (PAT)

The PAT is a hands-off, eyes-on tool. You assess all three sides simultaneously from the doorway — before you touch the child. It gives you an immediate gestalt of severity.

🧠
Appearance
Tone, interactivity, consolability, look/gaze, speech/cry
🫁
Work of Breathing
Sounds, position, retractions, nasal flaring, see-saw
🩸
Circulation to Skin
Pallor, mottling, cyanosis, skin colour & pattern

⏱ The 30-Second Rule

A complete PAT takes under 30 seconds. An abnormal PAT tells you to act immediately — before vitals, before monitors, before history. It buys you the time to do the rest properly.

PAT — Side 1

Appearance: the most sensitive indicator of CNS perfusion

TICLS Mnemonic

T

Tone

Moving spontaneously? Limp or stiff? Resists exam?

I

Interactivity

Alert? Playing? Aware of surroundings? Tracking visually?

C

Consolability

Calmed by caregiver? Inconsolable crying = major red flag.

L

Look / Gaze

Eye contact? Fixed stare? Glazed appearance?

S

Speech / Cry

Age-appropriate vocalization? Weak, high-pitched, or absent cry?

What Abnormal Appearance Means

🔴 Ominous Signs

No eye contact, limp tone, inconsolable, absent cry — these signal severely impaired CNS perfusion. This child needs immediate intervention regardless of other findings.

✅ Reassuring Signs

Crying vigorously, fixing gaze on parent, actively resisting exam — a child fighting you is rarely in imminent arrest.

PAT — Sides 2 & 3

Work of Breathing & Circulation to Skin

🫁 Work of Breathing

  • Abnormal sounds: Stridor (upper), wheeze (lower), grunting (auto-PEEP → severe), gurgling (secretions)
  • Abnormal positioning: Tripod, sniffing position, refusal to lie flat
  • Retractions: Subcostal, intercostal, suprasternal — depth ∝ severity
  • Nasal flaring: Infant sign of increased respiratory effort
  • See-saw breathing: Chest falls as abdomen rises = severe obstruction or failure

⚠️ Silent Chest

No wheeze + severe distress = near-total obstruction. More dangerous than loud wheeze.

🩸 Circulation to Skin

  • Pallor: Anaemia, poor perfusion, vasoconstriction
  • Mottling: Irregular pale/purple pattern = distributive or obstructive shock
  • Cyanosis: Central (lips/tongue) = hypoxaemia; peripheral only = poor perfusion or cold
  • Flushed/warm: Early distributive (septic) shock — skin may look deceptively normal

🔴 Central Cyanosis

Central cyanosis with normal or increased work of breathing = critical hypoxaemia. Oxygen and airway management immediately.

Primary Survey

ABCDE — systematic, head-to-toe, every time

A

Airway

Patent? Maintainable? Assess for obstruction (stridor, secretions, foreign body). Position: sniffing for infants, neutral for older. Jaw thrust if cervical injury suspected.

B

Breathing

Rate, depth, effort. SpO₂. Auscultate. Normal RR by age: Infant 30–60 · Toddler 24–40 · Preschool 22–34 · School 18–30 · Adolescent 12–16/min.

C

Circulation

HR, BP, cap refill (<2 sec normal), skin temp/color. Central vs peripheral pulses. Normal HR by age: Infant 100–160 · Toddler 90–150 · Preschool 80–140 · School 70–120 · Adolescent 60–100.

D

Disability

AVPU or GCS. Pupils (size, reactivity, symmetry). Blood glucose — always check in altered mental status. Posturing (decorticate/decerebrate = severe brain injury).

E

Exposure

Full skin inspection — rashes, petechiae, bruising, wounds. Temperature (core). Then cover to prevent hypothermia. Petechiae + fever = meningococcaemia until proven otherwise.

Disability — D in ABCDE

AVPU: rapid neurological assessment

A
Alert

Eyes open spontaneously. Responds appropriately to environment. Normal baseline for age.

V
Voice

Only responds when spoken to or called by name. Does not open eyes spontaneously.

P
Pain

Only responds to painful stimulus. GCS equivalent ~8. Airway at risk — act now.

U
Unresponsive

No response to any stimulus. GCS 3. Immediate airway management and resuscitation.

⚠️ P or U = Airway At Risk

Any child responding only to Pain or Unresponsive requires immediate airway positioning, BVM readiness, and consideration for advanced airway. Do not leave unattended.

💡 Blood Glucose — Always

Hypoglycaemia mimics every neurological emergency in children. Check BGL in every child with altered mental status before attributing to other causes.

Putting It Together

PAT findings → clinical severity → action

Stable

All PAT sides normal

Complete secondary survey. Monitor. Investigate underlying cause. Not time-critical.

Urgent

1–2 PAT sides abnormal

Respiratory distress OR poor perfusion OR altered appearance alone. Intervene while assessing. O₂, IV access, monitoring.

Immediate

All 3 PAT sides abnormal

Cardiopulmonary failure or arrest. Simultaneous resuscitation and assessment. Every second counts.

📋 Four Clinical States

Respiratory distress → Respiratory failure → Shock → Cardiopulmonary failure. Recognising the transition from distress to failure is the key PALS skill.

🔴 Compensated vs Decompensated Shock

Compensated shock: BP normal, HR elevated, cap refill prolonged. Decompensated: BP falls. Don't wait for hypotension — it's a late, pre-arrest sign in children.

Secondary Survey

SAMPLE history — once the child is stabilised

SAMPLE Mnemonic

S

Signs & Symptoms

Onset, duration, progression. What triggered the call?

A

Allergies

Medications, foods, latex. Any anaphylaxis history?

M

Medications

Current meds, recent changes, overdose possibility?

P

Past Medical History

Prematurity, cardiac disease, immunocompromise, seizures.

L

Last Oral Intake

Relevant for procedural sedation and surgical planning.

E

Events Leading Up

Trauma? Ingestion? Witnessed event? Fever duration?

Head-to-Toe Examination

  • Head: Fontanelle (bulging = raised ICP, sunken = dehydration), scalp injuries
  • Eyes: Pupils, scleral icterus, periorbital oedema
  • ENT: Tympanic membranes, oropharynx, neck stiffness
  • Chest: Auscultation (air entry symmetry), chest wall
  • Abdomen: Distension, tenderness, organomegaly, bowel sounds
  • Neuro: Tone, reflexes, cranial nerves, fontanelle
  • Skin: Full exposure — petechiae, purpura, bruising patterns

🔴 Non-Accidental Injury

Bruising in non-mobile infants, unusual patterns, inconsistent history. Document carefully. Mandatory reporting obligations apply.

Q

What are the 3 sides of the Pediatric Assessment Triangle?

Tap to reveal →

Appearance, Work of Breathing, Circulation to Skin. All assessed simultaneously, hands-off, from the doorway — in under 30 seconds.

Q

What does a "P" on the AVPU scale mean clinically?

Tap to reveal →

Responds only to Pain. GCS equivalent ~8. The airway is at immediate risk — position, prepare BVM, consider advanced airway.

Q

A child is tachycardic with prolonged cap refill but normal BP. What stage of shock?

Tap to reveal →

Compensated shock. BP is maintained by compensatory mechanisms. Act immediately — hypotension is a late pre-arrest sign. Don't wait for it.

Q

Silent chest in a child with severe respiratory distress — what does it mean?

Tap to reveal →

Near-total airway obstruction. No air movement = no wheeze. More dangerous than audible wheeze. Immediate BVM ventilation and airway management.

Clinical Pearl · Module 03

"The child who looks sick IS sick — trust the PAT."

Experienced clinicians often sense illness before they can name it. The PAT formalises that instinct into a reproducible, teachable 30-second framework. A child with abnormal appearance alone — even with normal vitals — deserves urgent assessment and cannot be safely triaged to a waiting room.

Module 03 — Summary

Assess systematically. Act before arrest.

👁 PAT Framework

  • Appearance (TICLS) · Work of Breathing · Circulation to Skin
  • Hands-off, 30-second doorway assessment
  • All 3 sides abnormal = cardiopulmonary failure — act immediately
  • Abnormal appearance alone = serious illness regardless of vitals

🔤 Primary Survey

  • ABCDE: Airway → Breathing → Circulation → Disability → Exposure
  • AVPU: P or U = airway at risk, act now
  • Always check blood glucose in altered mental status
  • Compensated shock: normal BP does NOT mean safe — treat early
