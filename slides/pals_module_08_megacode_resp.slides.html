Scenario Setup

3-Year-Old · Severe Asthma · 14 kg

Presentation
  • Setting: ED resuscitation room
  • 24-hour worsening wheeze and cough
  • 2 albuterol nebs at home, no improvement
  • No known allergies, no prior intubation
Initial Vitals
  • HR 162 · RR 52
  • SpO₂ 84% on room air
  • BP 92/56 · Temp 38.1°C
  • Cap refill 3 seconds
PAT — Paediatric Assessment Triangle

Appearance: Anxious, tripoding, head-bobbing  |  Work of breathing: Audible wheeze, intercostal and subcostal retractions  |  Circulation: Pale, mottled

🔴 Trigger Event (at 5 minutes)

After initial nebs + steroids: child becomes lethargic, SpO₂ drops to 72%, HR falls to 48 bpm. Rapid decompensation to respiratory failure with impending cardiac arrest.

Team Structure

Six-Person Resuscitation Team

Team Leader

Directs, prioritises, summarises, makes all definitive calls. Closes the loop on every order. Positions themselves where they can see the whole patient and the whole team.

Airway

Delivers O₂, manages BVM seal and rate, performs suction, prepares and executes advanced airway. Calls out chest rise with every breath.

Compressor

High-quality CPR at 100–120/min, ≥⅓ AP depth, full recoil. Rotates with the spare compressor every 2 minutes without a prompt.

Monitor / Defib

Attaches pads, calls rhythm clearly and loudly, manages defibrillator charge/delivery, calls ETCO₂ reading every cycle.

IV/IO + Meds

Obtains access, draws up and labels all medications, double-checks every dose with team leader before pushing, flushes after each drug.

Recorder

Documents times, doses, rhythm changes, and interventions. Calls 2-minute CPR cycles aloud: "2-minute cycle ends in 30 seconds."

Expected Actions

What "Excellent" Looks Like — Click to follow along

0:00
Immediate

Scene safety · PAT · Pulse + breath check ≤ 10 seconds

Simultaneous pulse (carotid/femoral) and breath check. Assign roles immediately. Apply NRB mask → 100% O₂. Call for resus team.

0:30
30 seconds

IV/IO access · Continuous nebulized albuterol · Steroids

IV methylprednisolone 2 mg/kg (28 mg). Albuterol 0.15 mg/kg neb continuous. Start ipratropium neb.

2:00
2 minutes

IV Magnesium sulfate · ETCO₂ waveform

Magnesium 25–50 mg/kg IV over 20 min (max 2 g). Apply ETCO₂ capnography. Reassess PAT. Prepare for possible intubation.

5:00
5 minutes — TRIGGER

Lethargy · SpO₂ 72% · HR 48 → Begin BVM ventilation

Switch to 2-rescuer BVM. 1 breath every 2–3 seconds. Adequate chest rise confirmed. Do not hyperventilate.

5:30
Critical branch

HR remains 48 + poor perfusion → START CPR

HR < 60 with signs of inadequate perfusion despite ventilation = CPR indication. Rate 100–120/min, 15:2 ratio (2-rescuer paediatric).

6:00
Drug phase

Epinephrine IV/IO · Prepare ETT 4.5 cuffed

Epi 0.01 mg/kg = 0.14 mg IV/IO. Repeat every 3–5 min. RSI drugs if intubating: ketamine 1–2 mg/kg + rocuronium 1 mg/kg.

8:00
ROSC

HR 132 · SpO₂ 96% · ETCO₂ 38 mmHg

Transition to post-arrest care: targeted oxygenation (SpO₂ 94–99%), normocapnia (ETCO₂ 35–45), avoid hyperthermia, PICU consult.

Auto-Fail Behaviours

These errors fail the megacode immediately

Airway / Breathing
  • Hyperventilation via BVM — raises intrathoracic pressure, impairs venous return, drops perfusion pressure
  • Inadequate BVM seal — visible chest rise required with every breath
  • Failing to call for advanced airway when BVM insufficient
CPR / Circulation
  • Delay starting CPR when HR < 60 with poor perfusion
  • CPR pauses longer than 10 seconds (rhythm check included)
  • Failing to rotate compressor every 2 minutes
  • Inadequate compression depth or incomplete recoil
Drugs / Doses
  • Wrong epinephrine concentration — 1:10,000 for IV/IO; 1:1,000 only for ET
  • Dose calculated on estimated weight without a Broselow tape or weight confirmation
  • Drugs pushed without double-check and verbal confirmation
Team Communication
  • No closed-loop communication — orders given but not confirmed
  • Team leader not positioned to see patient and team simultaneously
  • Recorder not calling 2-minute cycles aloud

Facilitator Guide

Plus / Delta Debrief — 15 Minutes

✓ Plus — What worked?
  • Crisp role assignments at scene entry
  • PAT completed and announced within 30 seconds
  • Deterioration recognised before full arrest
  • Time-stamped recorder calling cycles aloud
  • Correct epinephrine dose and concentration
Δ Delta — What to change?
  • BVM seal quality and ventilation rate
  • CPR pauses exceeding 10 seconds
  • Drug dose check-back missing or incomplete
  • Team leader not visible to full team
  • Compressor rotation not happening at 2 min

🎙 Facilitation technique

Allow learner self-reflection first before correcting. Ask: "What was your mental model at that moment?" Then ask: "What would you do differently?" Correct only what self-reflection misses.

📌 Teachable moments for this case

Hyperventilation in obstructed airways is lethal. The trapped air phenomenon in asthma — slow the rate down, let the chest fall. BVM rate in arrest = 1 breath every 6 seconds (10/min).

Knowledge Check

Tap each card to reveal the answer

Question

The 14 kg child has HR 48 despite O₂ and BVM ventilation for 30 seconds. What is your next action?

Tap to reveal →

Start CPR immediately. HR < 60 with signs of poor perfusion despite oxygenation = CPR indication in PALS. Do not wait. Rate 100–120/min, 15:2 two-rescuer ratio.

Question

What is the epinephrine dose for this 14 kg child and which concentration do you use?

Tap to reveal →

0.01 mg/kg = 0.14 mg IV/IO. Use 1:10,000 concentration (0.1 mL/kg = 1.4 mL). Never use 1:1,000 IV/IO — that is 10× too concentrated and reserved for the ET route only.

Question

Why is hyperventilation specifically dangerous in a child with severe asthma during BVM ventilation?

Tap to reveal →

Air trapping and dynamic hyperinflation increase intrathoracic pressure, impairing venous return and dropping cardiac output. Also causes hypocapnia-induced cerebral vasoconstriction. Use I:E ratio of 1:4 — slow, deliberate breaths, full exhalation.

Question

ETCO₂ reads 12 mmHg during CPR. What does this tell you and what should you do?

Tap to reveal →

ETCO₂ < 15 mmHg during CPR indicates poor cardiac output from inadequate compressions. Reassess rate, depth, recoil, and hand position. A sudden rise in ETCO₂ to >35 mmHg often signals ROSC before a pulse is palpable.

Clinical Pearl

"In paediatrics, the chest leads — the heart follows."

Most paediatric arrests are respiratory in origin. Fix the airway and restore oxygenation early and the bradycardia often reverses without ever needing a shock. Every second of hypoxia is seconds of brain. Airway first, always.
