🟡 The unifying principle

Every target above reduces secondary brain injury in the post-arrest period. ROSC is not the finish line — it is the starting gun for neurological rescue.

Respiratory Management

Avoid Hyperoxia and Hyperventilation

🪫 Oxygenation
  • Titrate FiO₂ to achieve SpO₂ 94–99% — wean from 100% as soon as ABG/SpO₂ allow
  • Hyperoxia worsens reperfusion injury through reactive oxygen species
  • Use pulse oximetry continuously; confirm with ABG
  • Target PaO₂ 75–100 mmHg on ABG if available
💨 Ventilation
  • Target PaCO₂ 35–45 mmHg — use ETCO₂ continuously to guide
  • Hypocapnia causes cerebral vasoconstriction and reduces cerebral blood flow
  • Avoid permissive hypercapnia unless specific indication (ARDS)
  • Normal rate for age; avoid breath-stacking and auto-PEEP

🔴 The hyperventilation trap

After ROSC, providers instinctively ventilate fast. PaCO₂ of 28 feels safe but causes cerebral ischaemia. Use waveform ETCO₂ and ABG to keep the number between 35 and 45 — every time.

Haemodynamic Support

Maintain Mean Perfusion Pressure

Fluids
  • Isotonic crystalloid 10 mL/kg bolus if hypovolaemic — reassess after each
  • Avoid excessive fluid — target euvolaemia not hypervolaemia
  • Target SBP ≥5th percentile for age
Monitoring
  • Arterial line: continuous BP + diastolic feedback
  • Central venous access for vasoactives and CVP
  • Early echocardiogram: myocardial dysfunction, effusion, congenital lesions
  • Correct electrolytes: K⁺ · Ca²⁺ · Mg²⁺

Temperature Management

Targeted Temperature Management — Avoid Fever Always

Phase
Protocol & Duration
🟡 Option A
Targeted Normothermia: maintain 36–37.5°C for at least 5 continuous days in unresponsive children
🔵 Option B
Therapeutic Hypothermia: cool to 32–34°C × 2 days → then rewarm to 36–37.5°C × 3 days — institutional protocol applies
🔴 All patients
Continuous core temperature monitoring (bladder or oesophageal probe) · Fever > 37.5°C must be treated immediately
⚪ Shivering
Treat with sedation, analgesia · NMB if refractory · Shivering increases metabolic demand and negates cooling

🔴 Never allow fever post-arrest

Even brief fever (>37.5°C) in the first 72 hours after ROSC is independently associated with worse neurological outcome. Treat it aggressively — antipyretics, cooling blanket, IV paracetamol.

Neurological Care

Anticipate and Prevent Secondary Injury

⚫ Continuous Monitoring
  • Pupillary check q1 hour — document bilaterally
  • Paediatric GCS or FOUR score q1–2 h
  • Continuous EEG when available — high subclinical seizure incidence post-arrest
  • Glucose Q1–2 h — treat hypoglycaemia immediately, avoid hyperglycaemia
  • Core temperature q1 h via invasive probe
🧠 Imaging & Interventions
  • Head CT: early if traumatic cause, herniation signs, or focal neuro deficit
  • MRI brain: most reliable prognostic tool — after rewarming, Day 5+
  • Treat seizures: levetiracetam first-line · phenytoin/fosphenytoin second
  • Consult neurology early
  • Prognostication discussion with family at 72 hours minimum

🟢 Prognostication window

Do not prognosticate neuro outcome before 72 hours after normothermia or rewarming. Sedation, hypothermia, and metabolic derangements confound all clinical and electrographic findings before this point.

Communication

Structured Handoff and Family Support

📋 PICU Handoff
  • Use SBAR + I-PASS structured tool — write it down before calling
  • Include: arrest timeline · downtime · CPR quality · shocks delivered · drugs given · total resus duration
  • State ROSC time, any re-arrests, code status, and family presence during CPR
  • Document all interventions with times in the medical record
👨‍👩‍👧 Family Communication
  • Family presence during resuscitation is supported with a dedicated facilitator
  • Early, honest communication: outcome uncertainty, role of TTM, neuro re-evaluation at 72 h
  • Avoid definitive prognosis in the first 24–48 hours
  • Activate social work, chaplaincy, palliative care as needed — early, not late

⚠️ Documentation is treatment

A complete, timestamped resuscitation record is essential for PICU handoff, quality review, and prognostication. If the arrest details aren't written down, they effectively didn't happen.

Knowledge Check

Tap each card to reveal the answer

Question 1

Your post-arrest 6-year-old has SpO₂ 99% on FiO₂ 100%. What do you do?

Tap to reveal →

Wean FiO₂ immediately. Target SpO₂ 94–99%. Maintaining 100% O₂ post-ROSC causes hyperoxia and worsens reperfusion injury through reactive oxygen species. Confirm with ABG (target PaO₂ 75–100 mmHg).

Question 2

How long must targeted normothermia be maintained in a comatose post-arrest child?

Tap to reveal →

At least 5 continuous days at 36–37.5°C. Alternatively, therapeutic hypothermia 32–34°C × 2 days then normothermia × 3 days. Core temperature monitoring via bladder or oesophageal probe throughout.

Question 3

When is it safe to give a family a neurological prognosis after paediatric cardiac arrest?

Tap to reveal →

Not before 72 hours after achieving normothermia or completing rewarming. Sedation, hypothermia, and metabolic derangements confound all clinical and electrographic findings before this window.

Question 4

ETCO₂ reads 28 mmHg on the ventilator. PaCO₂ on ABG is 29. What's your priority?

Tap to reveal →

Reduce ventilation rate immediately. PaCO₂ 29 mmHg = hypocapnia = cerebral vasoconstriction = reduced cerebral blood flow. Target PaCO₂ 35–45 mmHg. Use ETCO₂ for continuous guidance between ABGs.

Clinical Pearl
“The first 72 hours decide the next 72 years.”

ROSC is not the finish line — it is the starting gun for neurological rescue. Get oxygen, CO₂, perfusion, and temperature right, and the brain has its best shot. Prognostication before the 72-hour window is unreliable and harmful.
