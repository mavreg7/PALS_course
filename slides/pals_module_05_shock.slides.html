Module 05
Shock

Recognize shock early and treat the cause before decompensation.

Pediatric shock is usually compensated first. If clinicians wait for hypotension, the child is already in late shock. This module focuses on patterns, classification, and immediate stabilization priorities.

Definition

Shock = inadequate oxygen delivery to tissues

Oxygen delivery fails

Perfusion becomes insufficient for metabolic demand. Cells switch to anaerobic metabolism, lactate rises, organ dysfunction follows.

Blood pressure can stay normal

Children compensate with tachycardia and vasoconstriction. Hypotension is a late sign.

Clinical pearl

A child with altered mental status, tachycardia, prolonged capillary refill, weak distal pulses, and cool extremities may already be in significant shock even if the blood pressure looks acceptable.

Types

Main pediatric shock categories

Hypovolemic

Fluid loss: vomiting, diarrhea, bleeding, burns, dehydration.

Distributive

Maldistribution of flow: sepsis, anaphylaxis, neurogenic causes.

Cardiogenic

Pump failure: myocarditis, cardiomyopathy, congenital heart disease, arrhythmia.

Obstructive

Mechanical block: tamponade, tension pneumothorax, critical outflow obstruction, pulmonary embolism (rare).

Patterns

Warm vs cold shock pattern

Warm shock

Bounding pulses, flushed skin, wide pulse pressure, brisk cap refill, vasodilation.

VS

Cold shock

Weak pulses, cool extremities, delayed cap refill, narrow pulse pressure, vasoconstriction.

Either pattern may appear in septic shock depending on stage and physiology.

Assessment

Rapid bedside shock assessment

1

Mental status

Irritability, lethargy, decreased interaction, poor eye contact, confusion.

2

Perfusion

Cap refill, skin temperature, pulse quality, urine output, mottling, central vs distal pulses.

3

Breathing

Tachypnoea is common as compensation. Work of breathing may rise with acidosis.

4

Blood pressure

Helpful, but not sensitive early. Do not wait for hypotension before intervention.

Treatment priorities

Immediate management sequence

Airway and oxygen

Support airway, oxygenate, ventilate if needed. Reduce oxygen demand.

Vascular access

IV if fast, IO if delayed. Do not lose time in unstable shock.

Fluids if appropriate

Use isotonic boluses when hypovolemia is likely; reassess after each bolus.

Cause-directed therapy

Antibiotics, epinephrine, blood, decompression, antiarrhythmics, or cardiology input depending on mechanism.

Reassess constantly

Heart rate, pulses, BP, lung exam, cap refill, mental status, urine output.

Escalate early

If shock persists, call PICU/transport/support early and start vasoactive support when indicated.

Avoid harm

Common shock management mistakes

Waiting for hypotension

This delays recognition until the child is near decompensation.

Too much fluid in cardiogenic shock

May worsen pulmonary oedema and respiratory failure.

No reassessment after bolus

Every bolus needs a fresh exam before repeating.

Focusing on one number

Shock is a bedside pattern, not just a blood pressure problem.

Takeaway

Most important memory line

Tachycardia + abnormal perfusion + altered behavior = shock until proven otherwise. Treat early, reassess often, and tailor therapy to the underlying physiology.
