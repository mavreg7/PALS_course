Foundation
Why Algorithms Work
📈
Survival has improved
Pediatric IHCA survival increased from 19% (2000) to 44% (2022). Consistent algorithm use is central to this improvement.
🧠
Algorithms reduce cognitive load
In high-stress situations, structured decision trees prevent omissions and allow team members to anticipate next steps and prepare in parallel.
🔁
Two main pathways
Every cardiac arrest follows one of two branches: Shockable (VF/pVT) or Non-Shockable (Asystole/PEA). Rhythm check determines the path.
⏱️
Time is myocardium
Every 2-min CPR cycle matters. Minimize pauses (<10 sec). Change compressors every 2 min. Monitor ETCO₂ for CPR quality and ROSC signals.
Algorithm Core
Starting Every Arrest — Universal First Steps
1
Start CPR — Begin bag-mask ventilation with 100% oxygen. Attach monitor/defibrillator.
2
Check rhythm — Shockable (VF/pVT) or Non-Shockable (Asystole/PEA)? This single question determines every subsequent step.
3
Establish IV/IO access — as soon as possible. Do not interrupt CPR for access. IO is rapid, safe, and effective.
4
Consider advanced airway — ET tube or supraglottic airway. For OHCA, BMV is preferred over intubation (COR 2a). Use ETCO₂ to confirm placement.
5
Treat reversible causes (Hs & Ts) — simultaneously throughout the resuscitation. Every 2-min cycle = opportunity to reassess.
Shockable Pathway
VF / Pulseless VT — Step by Step
1
Shock ASAP — 2 J/kg. Resume CPR immediately after shock (do not check pulse first). 2 minutes of CPR.
2
Rhythm check — Still shockable? → Shock 4 J/kg. Establish IV/IO. Start CPR × 2 min. Give epinephrine 0.01 mg/kg every 3–5 min.
3
Rhythm check — Still shockable? → Shock 4 J/kg. CPR × 2 min. Give amiodarone 5 mg/kg OR lidocaine 1 mg/kg. Treat reversible causes.
4
Rhythm converts to non-shockable → Switch to asystole/PEA pathway. Continue epinephrine every 3–5 min.
5
ROSC achieved → Post-Cardiac Arrest Care (Module 08). Maintain SpO₂ 94–99%, normocapnia, blood pressure >10th percentile for age.
💡
For shockable rhythms: administer epinephrine after 2 defibrillation attempts, not before — prioritize early defibrillation. (COR 2b)
Non-Shockable Pathway
Asystole / PEA — Step by Step
1
Epinephrine ASAP — 0.01 mg/kg IV/IO. Earlier epinephrine in non-shockable arrest = better outcomes. Systematic review confirms shorter time to first dose → improved survival.
2
CPR × 2 min with IV/IO access. Consider advanced airway and ETCO₂. Repeat epinephrine every 3–5 min.
3
Rhythm check — If converts to shockable → shock and follow VF/pVT pathway. "Secondary VFpVT" is associated with WORSE outcomes than primary VF.
4
Still non-shockable → Continue CPR + epinephrine every 3–5 min + treat reversible causes. Reassess rhythm every 2 min.
5
No ROSC signs → Continue loop. If signs of ROSC → Post-Cardiac Arrest Care. Consider ECPR for refractory arrest with potentially reversible cause.
High-Quality CPR
CPR Quality Standards — 2025
💪
Push Hard
At least 1/3 of AP chest diameter. ~4 cm in infants, ~5 cm in children.
⏱️
Push Fast
100–120 compressions/min. Allow complete chest recoil between compressions.
⏸️
Minimize Pauses
Pauses <10 seconds. 2025 NEW: increased pause frequency AND duration → lower ROSC probability.
🔄
Change Compressors
Every 2 min or sooner if fatigued. Quality degrades rapidly after 2 min of compressions.
🌬️
Ventilation Ratio
15:2 if 2 rescuers + prepubescent. 30:2 if postpubescent or single rescuer. With advanced airway: continuous compressions + 1 breath every 2–3 sec.
📊
Monitor Feedback
ETCO₂ ≥20 mmHg correlates with ROSC. Diastolic BP target: ≥25 mmHg (infants), ≥30 mmHg (children ≥1yr) if arterial line available.
Drug Timing
Epinephrine — Timing and Dosing Principles
💊
Epinephrine increases coronary perfusion pressure via alpha-1 agonism. Dose: 0.01 mg/kg IV/IO (0.1 mL/kg of 0.1 mg/mL). Max: 1 mg. Every 3–5 min.
Non-shockable (Asystole/PEA)
Give epinephrine as early as possible. Meta-analysis: shorter time to 1st dose → improved favorable outcomes. Time <3 min = highest benefit. (COR 2a, LOE C-LD)
Shockable (VF/pVT)
Give epinephrine after 2 defibrillation attempts, or sooner only when rapid defibrillation is not possible. Adult data: early epi before shock = worse outcomes. (COR 2b, LOE C-LD)
!
Sodium bicarbonate: NOT recommended routinely (COR 3 No Benefit). Consider only in sodium channel blocker toxicity or hyperkalemia.
!
Calcium: NOT recommended routinely (COR 3 No Benefit). Meta-analysis shows decreased survival. Consider only in hypocalcemia, CCB overdose, or hyperkalemia.
Airway During CPR
Ventilation Strategy During Cardiac Arrest
😷
OHCA — BMV preferred
COR 2a: BMV is reasonable over intubation in OHCA. RCTs show similar neurologic outcomes. Avoids compressions interruption for intubation attempts.
🫁
IHCA — Either is reasonable
COR 2b: BMV or advanced airway placement (ETT/SGA) are both reasonable for IHCA. Clinical context determines best choice.
📈
With advanced airway
Continuous compressions + 1 breath every 2–3 sec (20–30/min). Avoid hyperventilation — worsens hemodynamics. Use ETCO₂ to confirm and monitor.
🎯
Ventilation rate target
20–30 breaths/min with advanced airway in place. Accounts for higher physiologic rate needs + avoids hemodynamic compromise from high rates.
⚠️
Hyperventilation during CPR = elevated intrathoracic pressure = decreased cardiac output. ETCO₂ monitoring helps prevent this.
Reversible Causes
The Hs & Ts — Identify and Treat
The Hs
Hypovolemia — Give fluid bolus 20 mL/kg NS/LR
Hypoxia — Optimize airway & 100% O₂
H⁺ (Acidosis) — Bicarb only if sodium channel blocker/hyperK
Hypoglycemia — Check glucose, give dextrose
Hypo/Hyperkalemia — ECG clues; treat if known
Hypothermia — Warm IV fluids, active rewarming
The Ts
Tension pneumothorax — Needle decompression 2nd ICS MCL
Tamponade (cardiac) — Pericardiocentesis
Toxins — Specific antidotes (naloxone, atropine, etc.)
Thrombosis (pulmonary) — Consider thrombolytics
Thrombosis (coronary) — Consider PCI/lytics
Vascular Access
IV vs IO — When and Why
💉
IV access preferred
Attempt IV first when feasible. Adults: IV preferred (2025 updated — IO associated with lower odds of ROSC vs IV in adults).
🦴
IO — rapid & reliable
IO is the go-to alternative when IV fails. Rapid (EZ-IO takes <30 sec), safe, effective for all PALS drugs. Works for epinephrine, amiodarone, fluids.
🚫
Endotracheal route NOT preferred
Poor drug absorption via ETT during CPR (limited pulmonary blood flow). If ETT is only access, doses must be 10× the IV dose for epinephrine.
⚖️
Decision factors
Choose based on availability, expertise, and timeliness — whichever can be established fastest without interrupting compressions. (COR 1, LOE C-EO)
💡
Weight-based dosing: Use actual weight when known. Broselow tape acceptable when weight unknown. Do not exceed adult maximum doses.
ECPR
Extracorporeal CPR — ECPR
💚
ECPR provides mechanical circulatory support when conventional CPR fails — particularly in patients with a potentially reversible etiology.
🔄
When to consider
Refractory cardiac arrest with reversible cause (myocarditis, toxic ingestion, pulmonary embolism, post-cardiac surgery). Select carefully.
🏥
Regionalized centers
ECPR is resource-intensive. 2025 new: Reasonable to develop regionalized ECPR programs with defined patient selection criteria to maximize survival and equity.
⏱️
Time matters
Shorter CPR-to-ECMO time = better outcomes. In myocarditis: <3h vs >6h from intubation to cannulation showed survival difference.
📊
Outcomes data
Myocarditis ECPR survival to discharge: 72%. General ECPR outcomes depend heavily on etiology. Non-cardiac causes have lower survival.
Traumatic Arrest
Cardiac Arrest from Trauma
🚨
Traumatic cardiac arrest management = standard resuscitation PLUS simultaneous trauma-specific interventions. Reversible causes must be corrected immediately.
1
Hemorrhage control — Direct pressure, tourniquets, wound packing. Restore circulating volume.
2
Tension pneumothorax — Needle decompression bilaterally if suspected. DO NOT wait for confirmation in arrest.
3
Cardiac tamponade — Pericardiocentesis or resuscitative thoracotomy for penetrating injuries.
4
Resuscitative thoracotomy — For penetrating thoracic injury with short transport time. May be reasonable (COR 2b). NOT indicated for blunt trauma arrest.
Team & Communication
Algorithm Execution as a Team
🗣️
Team leader role
Calls out rhythm, announces next drug, tracks time since last epinephrine, confirms 2-min cycles. Calls the algorithm out loud.
📋
Closed-loop communication
All orders confirmed and repeated back. "Epinephrine 1.5 mg IV given" not just "done." Prevents errors during high-stress resuscitation.
⏱️
Time keeping
Assign dedicated timekeeper. Announce every 2-min CPR cycle and epinephrine timing. ETCO₂ trending signals CPR quality and potential ROSC.
🔍
Hs & Ts checkup
At every 2-min rhythm check: verbally review Hs & Ts. Assign one team member to actively search for reversible causes.
💡
2025 New: Code teams should include ALS-trained members with clearly defined roles. Simulation-based training improves performance during actual arrests.
Clinical Scenario
Run the Algorithm
🏥 Scenario
An 8-year-old (25 kg) is found unresponsive on the ward. No pulse. Monitor shows coarse VF. IV access is already in place from a prior admission. Crash cart is at bedside. Timer starts.
Immediately (0 sec)
Start CPR. Attach defibrillator. Confirm VF. Charge to 50 J (2 J/kg × 25 kg).
Shock → CPR (0–2 min)
Shock 50 J. Resume CPR immediately. Do NOT check pulse right after shock. Continue 2 min of CPR.
Check rhythm (2 min)
Still VF → Shock 100 J (4 J/kg). Resume CPR. Give epinephrine 0.25 mg IV (0.01 mg/kg). Repeat every 3–5 min.
Check rhythm (4 min)
Still VF → Shock 100 J. Give amiodarone 125 mg (5 mg/kg) or lidocaine 25 mg. Continue CPR. Treat Hs & Ts.
