Fundamentals
Why Arrhythmias Matter in Pediatrics
🔁
Most arrests are NOT primary cardiac
Cardiac arrest in children typically results from progressive respiratory failure or shock — arrhythmias are often late findings. Early rhythm recognition prevents arrest.
📈
Shockable rhythms = better outcomes
VF/pVT are rarer in children than adults, but have better outcomes than asystole/PEA when defibrillated promptly. Recognition is time-critical.
⚡
Tachy vs Brady — different paths
Tachyarrhythmias are treated with vagal maneuvers, adenosine, or cardioversion. Bradyarrhythmias are treated with oxygenation, epinephrine, or pacing.
👶
Age-specific rate thresholds
Infants with SVT often run at ≥220 bpm; children ≥180 bpm. Sinus tachycardia rarely exceeds these rates. Rate pattern and P-wave morphology guide diagnosis.
Recognition
Sinus Tachycardia vs SVT
ℹ️
Sinus Tachycardia — P waves present & normal, RR interval variable, rate usually <220/min (infant), <180/min (child). History of fever, pain, hypovolemia.
Rate varies with patient state
P wave before every QRS
Treat the underlying cause
⚠️
SVT — P waves absent or abnormal, RR interval not variable, abrupt onset/offset, infant rate usually ≥220/min, child ≥180/min.
Sudden rate change history
Rate does NOT vary with activity
Requires active treatment
⚡
Key question: Is there cardiopulmonary compromise (altered mental status, signs of shock, hypotension)? This determines your treatment pathway.
SVT Treatment
SVT Without Cardiopulmonary Compromise
1
Vagal maneuvers first — ice water to face (infants), Valsalva or postural modification (children). Noninvasive, few adverse effects. Try first in stable patients. (COR 1, LOE C-LD)
2
Adenosine IV/IO if vagal maneuvers fail — 0.1 mg/kg rapid push (max 6 mg), flush immediately. Repeat at 0.2 mg/kg (max 12 mg). Fast onset, short half-life. (COR 1, LOE C-LD)
3
Expert consultation if adenosine fails — before adding second-line agents (procainamide, amiodarone, sotalol). Risk of proarrhythmia increases with multiple agents. (COR 1, LOE C-EO)
4
Second-line (if no expert available) — IV procainamide, amiodarone, or sotalol may be considered. (COR 2b, LOE C-LD)
💡
IV sotalol approved for pediatric SVT since 2009; 60–100% conversion rate in studies under electrophysiology guidance.
SVT Treatment
SVT With Cardiopulmonary Compromise
🚨
Signs of compromise: altered mental status, signs of shock, hypotension. Do NOT delay electrical therapy.
1
Synchronized cardioversion — 0.5–1 J/kg. If unsuccessful, increase to 2 J/kg. Sedate if possible but DO NOT DELAY shock. (COR 2a, LOE C-LD)
2
If IV/IO is in place, adenosine may be given while preparing for cardioversion — 0.1 mg/kg rapid push. Do not delay cardioversion for drug access.
3
Refractory to cardioversion + adenosine: consider procainamide, amiodarone, or sotalol only with extreme caution. Expert consultation strongly recommended.
⚠️
Use extreme caution when selecting and dosing sedatives in a compromised patient. Hemodynamic deterioration risk is high.
Wide Complex
Wide-Complex Tachycardia (WCT)
📊
QRS >0.09 sec with pulse
WCT in children is rare. Most common cause is SVT with aberrant conduction — NOT VT. Other causes: antidromic SVT, true VT.
🧠
Expert consultation first
For stable WCT — get expert guidance before antiarrhythmic drugs. Proarrhythmia risk is high (long QT, Brugada, WPW, cardiomyopathy). (COR 1, LOE C-LD)
1
Stable + regular monomorphic WCT: adenosine may be given diagnostically/therapeutically alongside expert consultation. If VT — shows VA dissociation. If SVT with aberrancy — may terminate. (COR 2a, LOE C-EO)
2
Unstable WCT (compromise): synchronized cardioversion 0.5–1 J/kg, escalate to 2 J/kg. Regardless of suspected origin. (COR 2a, LOE C-EO)
3
NEVER give adenosine to irregular WCT — may represent pre-excited atrial fibrillation; adenosine can precipitate VF.
Bradyarrhythmias
Bradycardia With a Pulse
⚠️
Bradycardia with cardiopulmonary compromise = immediate threat. Most pediatric bradycardia is caused by hypoxia — support the airway first.
1
Airway + oxygenation — Open airway, give 100% O₂, assist ventilation. This alone may resolve hypoxic bradycardia.
2
Cardiopulmonary compromise? — Altered mental status, hypotension, poor perfusion → proceed immediately with CPR if HR <60/min with signs of poor perfusion.
3
Epinephrine IV/IO — 0.01 mg/kg (0.1 mL/kg of 0.1 mg/mL) every 3–5 min. Increases rate via beta-1 and alpha-1 agonism.
4
Atropine IV/IO — 0.02 mg/kg (min 0.1 mg, max 0.5 mg) for increased vagal tone or primary AV block. Less useful for hypoxic bradycardia.
5
Cardiac pacing — for refractory bradycardia unresponsive to medications. Transcutaneous first, then transvenous if persistent.
Shockable Rhythms
VF and Pulseless VT — Defibrillation
⚡
VF/pVT = immediately shockable. Early defibrillation is the definitive treatment — the shorter the duration before shock, the better.
Shock #EnergyAction After

Amiodarone
5 mg/kg IV/IO bolus (max 300 mg). May repeat up to 3 doses (150 mg subsequent doses). For shock-refractory VF/pVT.
Lidocaine
1 mg/kg IV/IO. Alternative to amiodarone. Observational data shows higher ROSC and 24-hour survival with lidocaine vs amiodarone for IHCA.
Non-Shockable
Asystole and PEA
🔍
PEA (Pulseless Electrical Activity) = organized rhythm on monitor, but no pulse. Always search for and treat reversible causes — the Hs & Ts.
1
Start CPR immediately — Push hard (>1/3 AP diameter), fast (100–120/min), full recoil, minimize interruptions (<10 sec pauses).
2
Epinephrine ASAP — 0.01 mg/kg IV/IO. For non-shockable rhythms, earlier epinephrine is associated with improved outcomes. Every 3–5 min.
3
Treat reversible causes (Hs & Ts) — simultaneously with CPR. This is the primary driver of ROSC in PEA.
📋
Hs: Hypovolemia, Hypoxia, H⁺ (acidosis), Hypoglycemia, Hypo/Hyperkalemia, Hypothermia
Ts: Tension pneumothorax, Tamponade, Toxins, Thrombosis (pulmonary/coronary)

Synchronized cardioversion for SVT or WCT with compromise
⚡⚡
Escalated dose
2 J/kg
If initial 0.5–1 J/kg is unsuccessful
💊
Sedation
Give before cardioversion if time permits. Use extreme caution in compromised patients. Do NOT delay shock for sedation.
🔄
Synchronized vs Defibrillation: Cardioversion is synchronized to the R-wave to avoid delivering shock on T-wave (which could induce VF). Defibrillation is asynchronous — for VF/pVT only.
Special Populations
Myocarditis & Cardiomyopathy
🚨
Fulminant myocarditis: cardiac arrest incidence 25–75%. Ventricular arrhythmias requiring treatment are strongly associated with mortality (OR 8.47). ICU transfer is critical.
🏥
Early ICU transfer
For any child with myocarditis + arrhythmias, heart block, ST changes, or low cardiac output. Do not wait for deterioration. (COR 1, LOE B-NR)
🔧
ECMO/MCS consideration
Refractory low cardiac output → early ECMO/MCS. Transplant-free survival in myocarditis patients supported with ECMO: 72–80%. Early cannulation associated with survival.
⚡
ECPR for cardiac arrest
Early ECPR in myocarditis cardiac arrest is reasonable. 72% survival to discharge with ECPR vs 65% without. (COR 2a, LOE B-NR)
🔄
Good prognosis potential
Unlike congenital heart disease, myocarditis has high potential for partial/complete recovery of myocardial function with mechanical support.
Decision Framework
Tachyarrhythmia Algorithm — At a Glance
1
Assess stability — Altered mental status? Shock signs? Hypotension? → YES = compromised, NO = stable
2
Measure QRS — Narrow (<0.09s) = likely SVT | Wide (≥0.09s) = possible VT or SVT with aberrancy
3
Stable narrow QRS (SVT) → Vagal maneuvers → Adenosine → Expert consultation
4
Compromised narrow QRS (SVT) → Adenosine (if IV ready) OR synchronized cardioversion 0.5–1 J/kg
5
Stable wide QRS → Expert consultation; adenosine only if monomorphic & regular
6
Compromised wide QRS → Synchronized cardioversion immediately, regardless of origin
7
Pulseless (VF/pVT) → Defibrillation 2 J/kg → CPR → escalate per cardiac arrest algorithm
Clinical Scenario
Apply Your Knowledge
🏥 Scenario
A 3-year-old (14 kg) is brought in by parents who say he "suddenly became pale and was crying and then went quiet." On exam: HR 235 bpm, BP 70/40, RR 42, SpO₂ 92%. He is limp, minimally responsive to your voice. Monitor shows narrow QRS at 235 bpm with no discernible P waves. You have IV access.
What rhythm is this?
SVT — narrow complex, rate 235, no P waves, no rate variability
Is he compromised?
YES — hypotension, altered mental status, pallor, poor perfusion
Immediate action?
Synchronized cardioversion 0.5–1 J/kg (7–14 J). Sedate if time permits. Adenosine 1.4 mg IV if IV already running while preparing shock.
After successful conversion?
Admit to PICU, cardiology consult, 12-lead ECG, search for underlying cause (WPW, myocarditis, electrolytes)
