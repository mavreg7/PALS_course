Module 04
Respiratory

Respiratory failure is the leading cause of pediatric cardiac arrest.

Most pediatric arrests are respiratory in origin — not primarily cardiac. Recognizing and treating respiratory failure early is the single most impactful intervention available to any PALS provider.

The spectrum

Respiratory distress → failure → arrest

Respiratory distress

Increased work of breathing. Child is compensating. Airway open, oxygenating, but using accessory muscles, nasal flaring, or retracting.

Respiratory failure

Compensation is failing. Oxygenation or ventilation is impaired despite effort. Altered mental status, fatigue, cyanosis, decreasing respiratory rate may appear.

Respiratory arrest

No effective respiratory effort. Apnea or agonal breathing. Immediate intervention required or cardiac arrest follows within minutes.

Key principle

A child who was working hard to breathe and suddenly becomes quiet and limp is not improving — they are exhausting. Decreasing work of breathing with deteriorating mental status = impending failure.

Classification

Types of respiratory problems

Upper airway obstruction

Croup, epiglottitis, foreign body, anaphylaxis, peritonsillar abscess. Stridor is the hallmark. Maintain position of comfort, avoid agitation, prepare for definitive airway.

Lower airway obstruction

Asthma, bronchiolitis, foreign body aspiration distal. Expiratory wheeze, prolonged expiration, air trapping. Bronchodilators, heliox in selected cases.

Lung tissue disease

Pneumonia, pulmonary oedema, ARDS, contusion. Crackles, decreased breath sounds, hypoxia disproportionate to work of breathing. Oxygen, ventilation support.

Disordered control

Neuromuscular disease, CNS depression (poisoning, seizure, head injury), apnoea of prematurity. Shallow/irregular rate, poor tone. Airway positioning, bag-mask ventilation.

Severity

Mild, moderate, severe — rapid assessment

Mild distress

  • Alert, interactive
  • Mild retractions
  • SpO₂ ≥94% on room air
  • Normal or slightly elevated RR
  • Speaking normally

Moderate distress

  • Anxious, less interactive
  • Moderate retractions
  • SpO₂ 90–93% on room air
  • Significant tachypnoea
  • Short sentences only

Severe / failure

  • Lethargic, agitated, or obtunded
  • Severe retractions or paradoxical breathing
  • SpO₂ <90% despite oxygen
  • Bradypnoea or apnoea
  • Unable to speak or cry
Assessment signs

What to look for on exam

Work of breathing signs

  • Nasal flaring
  • Subcostal, intercostal, suprasternal retractions
  • Head bobbing (infants)
  • Seesaw breathing (paradoxical chest-abdomen)
  • Tripod positioning / sniffing position

Airway sounds

  • Stridor — upper airway narrowing
  • Wheeze — lower airway obstruction
  • Grunting — auto-PEEP, maintains alveolar patency
  • Gurgling — secretions, fluid in upper airway
  • Silence with distress — near-complete obstruction

Oxygenation

  • Central cyanosis (lips, mucous membranes)
  • SpO₂ — target ≥94%
  • Remember: SpO₂ may stay normal until significant hypoxia

Mental status changes

  • Irritability or agitation (early hypoxia)
  • Lethargy, poor eye contact (worsening)
  • Obtunded, unresponsive (critical)
Management

Immediate treatment priorities

Position

Sniffing position for infants. Upright position of comfort for older children with UAO. Never force a child with epiglottitis to lie down.

Oxygen

High-flow O₂ for all significant distress. Target SpO₂ ≥94%. Use non-rebreather for severe cases. Humidified when possible.

Bag-mask ventilation

For inadequate breathing effort, apnoea, or failure to maintain SpO₂. Correct mask seal and airway positioning are essential.

Suction

Clear secretions from upper airway. Bulb syringe for infants. Suction catheter for older children. Gentle — avoid vagal stimulation in neonates.

Cause-specific Rx

Nebulised salbutamol (asthma/bronchiolitis), epinephrine (croup/anaphylaxis), dexamethasone (croup), antibiotics (bacterial pneumonia/epiglottitis).

Advanced airway

RSI and endotracheal intubation when bag-mask ventilation is inadequate or prolonged support is needed. Surgical airway if cannot intubate/oxygenate.

Avoid harm

Common respiratory management mistakes

Agitating a child with UAO

Crying increases oxygen demand and can precipitate complete obstruction in epiglottitis or severe croup. Keep calm, keep parent present, avoid unnecessary interventions.

Misreading improving RR

A slowing respiratory rate with worsening consciousness is respiratory fatigue — it is not improvement. Intervene immediately.

Inadequate mask seal

Poor BMV technique delivers far less tidal volume than intended. Practice the EC-clamp technique and confirm chest rise with every breath.

Delaying definitive airway

If bag-mask ventilation is inadequate and the child is deteriorating, call for help and establish a definitive airway early — not after arrest.

Takeaway

The single most important rule

If a child has signs of respiratory distress, act before failure develops. Oxygenate, position, support breathing, and treat the underlying cause. Early intervention prevents the respiratory → cardiac arrest spiral.
