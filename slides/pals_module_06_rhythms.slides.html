Bradyarrhythmias

HR < 60 with poor perfusion → start CPR

Sinus Bradycardia

Regular, slow, P before every QRS. Treat if perfusion is poor or HR <60.

Complete (3°) AV Block

Cyan — P-waves. Red — QRS. Completely dissociated. Treat cause, consider pacing.

🚨 PALS Bradycardia Rule

If HR <60 and signs of poor perfusion despite oxygenation & ventilation → start CPR, give epinephrine. Don’t wait for asystole.

Tachyarrhythmias

SVT vs Sinus Tachycardia

Sinus Tachycardia
  • Rate varies — R-R interval changes
  • P-wave before every QRS
  • Usually <220 bpm (infant) / <180 (child)
  • History: fever, pain, dehydration, SVT history absent
  • Responds to treating the cause
VS
SVT
  • Rate fixed — R-R interval constant
  • P-waves absent or abnormal (retrograde)
  • ≥220 bpm (infant) / ≥180 (child)
  • Abrupt onset and termination
  • No history of fever/illness
SVT Management

Stable → Vagal → Adenosine → Cardiovert

Step 1 — Stable

Vagal Manoeuvres

  • Ice water to face (infant) — diver’s reflex
  • Valsalva (older child: blow through narrow straw)
  • Do NOT use ocular pressure in children
Step 2 — Stable/Unstable

Adenosine IV/IO

  • 0.1 mg/kg rapid IV push (max 6 mg)
  • Second dose: 0.2 mg/kg (max 12 mg)
  • Use proximal large vein + rapid flush
  • Expect brief asystole; warn family
Step 3 — Unstable

Synchronised Cardioversion

  • 0.5–1 J/kg (sync on R-wave)
  • Second shock: 2 J/kg
  • Sedate if possible but don’t delay if unstable
  • Never use unsynchronised for a perfusing rhythm

⚡ Adenosine Tip

Give with a 3-way tap: adenosine in one port, large flush in another. Push both simultaneously for fastest delivery to the SA node. A slow push won’t work — its half-life is <10 seconds.

Ventricular Arrhythmias

Wide complex = worry until proven otherwise

Monomorphic VT
  • Wide QRS (>0.09 sec in children)
  • Regular, monomorphic complexes
  • May have pulse (treat with cardioversion) or pulseless (defibrillate)
Ventricular Fibrillation
  • Chaotic, irregular, no recognisable QRS
  • No pulse — immediately shockable
  • Defibrillate 2 J/kg → 4 J/kg → ≥4 J/kg
Non-Shockable Rhythms

No shock. CPR + epinephrine + reversible causes

Asystole
  • Flat line — no electrical activity
  • Confirm in ≥2 leads before calling
  • Most common arrest rhythm in infants
  • CPR + epinephrine + treat reversibles
PEA (Pulseless Electrical Activity)
  • Organised electrical activity — no pulse
  • Always search for reversible cause (Hs & Ts)
  • CPR + epinephrine q3–5 min
  • Treat underlying: tension PTX, tamponade, hypovolaemia
Branch Point

The single most important question at every arrest

⚡ Shockable

VF · Pulseless VT

  • Defib 2 J/kg → resume CPR immediately
  • After 2 min CPR, reassess → 4 J/kg
  • Subsequent shocks ≥4 J/kg (max 10 J/kg)
  • Epinephrine after 2nd shock, then q3–5 min
  • Amiodarone 5 mg/kg after 3rd shock (or lidocaine)
⚪ Non-Shockable

Asystole · PEA

  • High-quality CPR immediately
  • Epinephrine 0.01 mg/kg (0.1 mL/kg of 1:10,000) q3–5 min
  • Search and treat reversible causes (Hs & Ts)
  • Reassess rhythm every 2 minutes

⏱ Minimise Interruptions

Rhythm checks should be <10 seconds. Deliver shocks during compressions if a biphasic defibrillator is available. Every second of no-flow time reduces survival.

Knowledge Check

Tap each card to reveal the answer

Q

Infant HR 280 bpm, fixed R-R interval, no P-waves. Diagnosis?

Tap to reveal →

SVT. Rate ≥220 in infants, fixed interval, absent P-waves = SVT. First try vagal manoeuvres, then adenosine 0.1 mg/kg rapid IV push.

Q

Organised QRS on monitor, no pulse. What rhythm, what action?

Tap to reveal →

PEA. CPR immediately, epinephrine 0.01 mg/kg q3-5 min, search Hs & Ts (tension PTX, tamponade, hypovolaemia are most common in children).

Q

First defibrillation dose in a child with VF?

Tap to reveal →

2 J/kg. Second shock 4 J/kg. Subsequent ≥4 J/kg (max 10 J/kg or adult max dose). Resume CPR immediately after every shock — don’t pause to check rhythm.

Q

P-waves and QRS are completely dissociated. Diagnosis and key treatment?

Tap to reveal →

Complete (3°) AV block. Treat cause, consider transcutaneous pacing, epinephrine or isoproterenol infusion. Atropine is rarely effective.

Clinical Pearl

“Treat the patient, then the strip.”

A rhythm on a monitor doesn’t tell you if the patient has a pulse, is perfusing, or is in pain. Always check the patient first. A 3° AV block with a happy child playing is managed very differently from the same rhythm in a grey, limp infant.
