Bradycardia Algorithm

Click each step to expand the treatment detail

1

Identify bradycardia with cardiopulmonary compromise

Poor perfusion, hypotension, respiratory distress, altered consciousness — any of these in combination with HR below normal for age.

Clinical markers: pallor, mottling, cap refill >3 sec, irritability or decreased LOC, hypotension for age.
2

Support ABCs, O₂, monitoring, IV/IO access

Open airway, give high-flow oxygen, attach cardiac monitor and pulse oximeter, establish vascular access.

Don’t delay: these steps happen simultaneously in 30 seconds. One team member airway, one IV access, one monitor.
3

HR < 60 + poor perfusion despite O₂ & ventilation?

This is the branch point. If the answer is YES — start CPR immediately.

Yes → start CPR (100-120/min, 15:2 with 2 rescuers). No → observe and treat reversible cause; CPR not yet indicated.
4

Epinephrine

First-line drug for bradycardia not responsive to oxygenation.

Epinephrine 0.01 mg/kg IV/IO (0.1 mL/kg of 1:10,000). Repeat q3–5 min. Max single dose 1 mg. If no IV/IO, ET route 0.1 mg/kg of 1:1,000 (10× concentration).
5

Atropine (if vagal tone or AV block)

Second-line for bradycardia with high vagal tone or AV conduction block.

Atropine 0.02 mg/kg IV/IO. Min dose 0.1 mg (avoid paradoxical bradycardia), max single dose 0.5 mg. May repeat once. Not effective for complete AV block.
6

Treat reversible causes (Hs & Ts)

Hypoxia, hypothermia, hypovolaemia, hypo/hyperkalaemia, hydrogen ion — tension PTX, tamponade, toxins, thromboembolism.

Most common in children: hypoxia (fix first), hypovolaemia (fluid), hypothermia (rewarm), toxins/drug ingestion (consider naloxone, atropine).
7

Consider transcutaneous pacing

For high-degree AV block or sinus node dysfunction not responding to medications.

Rate 100 bpm initially. Increase milliamps until capture (electrical + mechanical). Sedate for pain. Arrange transvenous pacing with expert consultation.

Adenosine Technique

Half-life 10 seconds — every second of delivery counts

Setup
  • Use the largest, most proximal IV (antecubital > hand)
  • Prepare a 3-way stopcock with adenosine in one port and 10 mL saline flush in the other
  • Both syringes drawn up and labelled before you start
  • Continuous rhythm strip running; team briefed on expected asystole
Delivery
  • Push adenosine as fast as possible then immediately flush with the saline — close the stopcock to the patient port and push both simultaneously
  • Elevate the arm to speed delivery to central circulation
  • If no response after 1–2 min, give second dose 0.2 mg/kg (max 12 mg)
  • If IO route: same technique, equally effective

⚠️ What to expect

Brief sinus pause or asystole on the monitor (seconds). Warn family. Have atropine and pacing ready. If pause is prolonged (>10 sec), prepare to pace. The rhythm that follows reveals the diagnosis: if sinus tach returns, that was the mechanism; if different, reassess.

Energy Selection

Sync or async — choosing the wrong one can kill

⚡ Synchronized Cardioversion

Shock delivered ON the R-wave

  • Use for: Unstable SVT, VT with pulse
  • Starting dose: 0.5–1 J/kg
  • Second shock: 2 J/kg
  • Sedate/analgesia if time allows (midazolam + fentanyl)
  • Confirm “sync” mode is ON before charging
  • Sync marker should appear on each QRS peak on monitor

🔍 Why sync?

An unsynchronised shock landing on the T-wave can induce VF. Always sync for organised rhythms with a pulse.

⚡ Defibrillation (Unsynchronised)

Shock delivered immediately

  • Use for: VF, Pulseless VT, Polymorphic VT
  • First dose: 2 J/kg
  • Second dose: 4 J/kg
  • Subsequent: ≥4 J/kg (max 10 J/kg or adult max)
  • Resume CPR immediately after shock — no rhythm check pause
  • Epinephrine after 2nd shock, then q3–5 min

⏱ Minimise no-flow time

Chest compressions should continue until the defibrillator is charged. Clear the patient for ≤1 second. Resume CPR within 5 seconds of shock delivery.

2025 Update

IV Sotalol — New Addition for Refractory SVT

2025 PALS Guidelines

What changed?

IV sotalol (1 mg/kg over 5 min) is now listed as an acceptable option for stable SVT that has not responded to adenosine, as an alternative to or alongside amiodarone and procainamide.

Clinical context

  • For SVT refractory to 2 doses of adenosine
  • May also be considered for atrial flutter/fibrillation in paediatric patients
  • Strong evidence base from paediatric electrophysiology

Monitoring requirements

  • Continuous 12-lead or rhythm monitoring during infusion
  • Watch for QTc prolongation — stop if QTc >500 ms
  • Blood pressure monitoring: sotalol has negative inotropic effect
  • Expert consultation strongly recommended

🧠 Why it matters

Previously, refractory SVT options were limited to amiodarone (slow infusion, phlebitis risk) or procainamide (hypotension). IV sotalol adds a class III option with good paediatric safety data and a faster onset.

Knowledge Check

Tap each card to reveal the answer

Q

4-year-old, HR 240, no P-waves, poor perfusion. Adenosine not immediately available. What energy for cardioversion?

Tap to reveal →

0.5–1 J/kg synchronised. Unstable SVT with compromised perfusion — don’t wait for adenosine. Sync cardioversion immediately, sedate if time permits. Second shock 2 J/kg.

Q

Why is there a minimum dose of 0.1 mg for atropine in children?

Tap to reveal →

Sub-therapeutic doses of atropine can cause paradoxical bradycardia by stimulating presynaptic muscarinic receptors. Always give at least 0.1 mg regardless of weight calculation.

Q

You try to cardiovert unstable VT but the defibrillator keeps delaying the shock. Why?

Tap to reveal →

The defibrillator is in sync mode, waiting for an R-wave to trigger the shock. In polymorphic VT or VF there is no clear R-wave — switch to unsynchronised (defib) mode and shock immediately.

Q

What two antiarrhythmics must NEVER be combined in paediatric tachycardia management?

Tap to reveal →

Amiodarone + Procainamide. Both prolong the QT interval. Combining them dramatically increases the risk of torsades de pointes and lethal arrhythmia. Choose one or the other.

Clinical Pearl

“Find the cause, then chase the rhythm.”

Sinus tachycardia from fever won’t respond to adenosine. Bradycardia from hypoxia won’t respond to atropine. Treating only the rhythm without addressing the driver is a recipe for recurrence. Always ask: why is this heart beating like this?
