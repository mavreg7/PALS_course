The Systematic Assessment

The difference between a good and a great clinician is a structured assessment that catches deterioration before arrest. PAT → Primary → Secondary — every time, every patient.

The Framework

Three-phase assessment — from doorway to diagnosis

1

PAT — Pediatric Assessment Triangle (Doorway Assessment)

A 30-second visual assessment from across the room before you touch the child. Identifies sick vs not sick instantly. Uses Appearance, Work of Breathing, and Circulation to Skin.

2

Primary Survey — ABCDE

Hands-on systematic evaluation: Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability (neuro), Exposure. Identify and treat life threats simultaneously as you find them — don't defer treatment.

3

Secondary Survey — Head-to-Toe

Complete head-to-toe examination, history (SAMPLE), and focused diagnostics after life threats are managed. Identifies underlying cause and guides definitive treatment.

🔑 The Golden Rule

Never move to the next phase if a life-threatening problem has been found but not addressed. The framework is iterative — reassess after every intervention.

Phase 1 — Doorway

The Pediatric Assessment Triangle

Tone, Interactiveness, Consolability, Look/Gaze, Speech/Cry

TICLS mnemonic

🫁

Work of Breathing

Abnormal sounds, abnormal positioning, retractions, nasal flaring, head bobbing

🩸

Circulation to Skin

Pallor, mottling, cyanosis — assessed visually before touching the child

⚡ Why PAT First?

The PAT gives you a global picture in 30 seconds without touching the child — which avoids distress that changes your exam findings. A child who is well-appearing, no increased work of breathing, and normal skin colour is almost never critically ill.

PAT Interpretation

What the PAT pattern tells you

Abnormal Appearance Only

Primary CNS / Metabolic

Altered mental status without respiratory or circulatory signs. Think: hypoglycaemia, seizure, head injury, toxic ingestion, meningitis. Airway protective reflexes may be compromised.

Abnormal Work of Breathing Only

Respiratory Problem

Child is alert and compensating. Early respiratory distress — asthma, croup, bronchiolitis, foreign body. Act before fatigue causes decompensation.

Abnormal Circulation Only

Compensated Shock

Pale/mottled skin but alert and breathing normally — early hypovolaemic or distributive shock. Compensatory mechanisms are working but will fail.

All Three Abnormal

Cardiopulmonary Failure

Pre-arrest state. Immediate intervention required. Call for help now, prepare for CPR. Do not wait for the full survey.

Phase 2 — Primary Survey

ABCDE — find and fix life threats immediately

A

Airway

Is it open and maintainable? Look for stridor, gurgling, snoring. Position, suction, jaw thrust, airway adjunct, or definitive airway as indicated. Obstruction kills in minutes.

B

Breathing

RR, SpO₂, auscultation, accessory muscle use, tidal volume. Normal RR is age-dependent. SpO₂ <94% on room air requires supplemental O₂. Attach pulse ox immediately.

C

Circulation

HR, BP, cap refill, pulses (central vs peripheral), skin colour/temp. Normal cap refill ≤2 sec. Absent peripheral pulses = decompensated shock. Get IV/IO now.

D

Disability (Neurological)

Level of consciousness (AVPU or GCS), pupils, blood glucose (always). Any altered consciousness → check glucose immediately. Posturing = severe CNS pathology.

E

Exposure

Undress the child fully — rashes (petechiae/purpura = meningococcal until proven otherwise), trauma signs, abdominal distension, temperature. Cover after assessment to prevent hypothermia.

Disability — Neuro Assessment

AVPU — rapid consciousness scale

A
Alert
Awake, aware, responds appropriately to environment
V
Voice
Responds only to verbal stimulation — eyes open or verbal response
P
Pain
Responds only to painful stimulus. Equivalent to GCS ~8. Airway at risk.
U
Unresponsive
No response to any stimulus. Airway unprotected — act immediately.

🩺 Always Check Glucose

Hypoglycaemia is the single most common reversible cause of altered consciousness in children. Check it during every "D" — never assume it's normal.

👁 Pupils

PERRL = reassuring. Unequal, fixed, or dilated pupils suggest herniation, toxidrome, or direct eye/nerve injury. Always document baseline.

Phase 3 — Secondary Survey

SAMPLE history + head-to-toe exam

SAMPLE History

  • Symptoms — onset, duration, progression
  • Allergies — medications, foods, environment
  • Medications — doses, last given, new meds
  • Past medical history — prior admissions, chronic illness
  • Last oral intake — timing matters for airway management
  • Events leading up — trauma, fever, exposures, ingestions

Head-to-Toe Exam Focus Points

  • Head: fontanelle (infants), scalp trauma, Battle's sign
  • Eyes: pupils, periorbital bruising, conjunctival pallor
  • Neck: JVD, tracheal deviation, lymphadenopathy
  • Chest: symmetry, auscultation, rib tenderness
  • Abdomen: tenderness, rigidity, organomegaly, distension
  • Extremities: perfusion, deformity, oedema
  • Skin: petechiae, purpura, burns, bruising patterns

🚨 Non-Accidental Trauma Flag

Bruising in pre-mobile infants, patterned bruises, retinal haemorrhages, and inconsistent histories must trigger child protection pathways immediately.

Knowledge Check

Tap each card to reveal the answer

Q

What does the PAT assess and how long should it take?

Tap to reveal →

Appearance, Work of Breathing, Circulation to Skin. Done visually in ~30 seconds from the doorway before touching the child. TICLS for Appearance.

Q

PAT shows abnormal appearance only — what's the likely problem category?

Tap to reveal →

Primary CNS or metabolic problem — hypoglycaemia, seizure, toxic ingestion, meningitis. No respiratory or circulatory abnormality but brain function is impaired.

Q

At what AVPU level is the airway at risk?

Tap to reveal →

P (Pain) and below. P is approximately GCS 8 — protective airway reflexes are unreliable. U (Unresponsive) requires immediate airway intervention.

Q

What must you ALWAYS check during "D" of the primary survey?

Tap to reveal →

Blood glucose. Hypoglycaemia is the most common reversible cause of altered consciousness in children and is missed if not specifically checked.

Clinical Pearl · Module 03

"The child who looks well — is well. Trust the PAT."

A child with a normal appearance (interactive, appropriate tone, consolable), no increased work of breathing, and normal skin colour has an enormous physiological reserve. The PAT that's all normal lets you slow down and think. The PAT with all three abnormal means you act before you assess further.

Module 03 — Summary

Structured assessment saves lives

  • Appearance — TICLS (Tone, Interact, Consolable, Look, Speech)
  • Work of Breathing — sounds, position, retractions, flaring
  • Circulation to Skin — pallor, mottling, cyanosis

  • Airway — open? maintainable?
  • Breathing — RR, SpO₂, auscultation
